## Abstract

Sign language is a visual language that encompasses all linguistic features of natural languages and serves as the primary communication method for the deaf and hard-of-hearing communities. Although many studies have successfully adapted pretrained language models (LMs) for sign language translation (sign-to-text), the reverse task-sign language generation (text-to-sign)-remains largely unexplored. In this work, we introduce a multilingual sign language model, Signs as Tokens (SOKE), which can generate 3D sign avatars autoregressively from text inputs using a pretrained LM. To align sign language with the LM, we leverage a decoupled tokenizer that discretizes continuous signs into token sequences representing various body parts. During decoding, unlike existing approaches that flatten all part-wise tokens into a single sequence and predict one token at a time, we propose a multi-head decoding method capable of predicting multiple tokens simultaneously. This approach improves inference efficiency while maintaining effective information fusion across different body parts. To further ease the generation process, we propose a retrieval-enhanced SLG approach, which incorporates external sign dictionaries to provide accurate word-level signs as auxiliary conditions, significantly improving the precision of generated signs. Extensive qualitative and quantitative evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of SOKE.

A multilingual sign language model, Signs as Tokens (SOKE), which can generate 3D sign avatars autoregressively from text inputs using a pretrained LM is introduced and a retrieval-enhanced SLG approach is proposed, which incorporates external sign dictionaries to provide accurate word-level signs as auxiliary conditions, significantly improving the precision of generated signs.

# Signs as Tokens: An Autoregressive Multilingual Sign Language Generator

Ronglai Zuo   Rolandos Alexandros Potamias   Evangelos Ververas Jiankang Deng   Stefanos Zafeiriou Imperial College London {r.zuo, r.potamias, e.ververas16, j.deng16, s.zafeiriou}@imperial.ac.uk

###### Abstract

Sign language is a visual language that encompasses all linguistic features of natural languages and serves as the primary communication method for the deaf and hard-of-hearing communities. While many studies have successfully adapted pretrained language models (LMs) for sign language translation (sign-to-text), drawing inspiration from its linguistic characteristics, the reverse task of sign language generation (SLG, text-to-sign) remains largely unexplored. Most existing approaches treat SLG as a visual content generation task, employing techniques such as diffusion models to produce sign videos, 2D keypoints, or 3D avatars based on text inputs, overlooking the linguistic properties of sign languages. In this work, we introduce a multilingual sign language model, Signs as Tokens (SOKE), which can generate 3D sign avatars autoregressively from text inputs using a pretrained LM. To align sign language with the LM, we develop a decoupled tokenizer that discretizes continuous signs into token sequences representing various body parts. These sign tokens are integrated into the raw text vocabulary of the LM, allowing for supervised fine-tuning on sign language datasets. To facilitate multilingual SLG research, we further curate a large-scale Chinese sign language dataset, CSL-Daily, with high-quality 3D pose annotations. Extensive qualitative and quantitative evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of SOKE. The project page is available at [https://2000zrl.github.io/soke/](https://2000zrl.github.io/soke/) .

## 1 Introduction

Sign language is the primary form of communication method utilized by the deaf and hard-of-hearing communities, embodying all linguistic properties found in spoken languages, including discrete semantic units and grammatical structure [ [68](#bib.bib68) , [13](#bib.bib13) ] .

However, despite its importance, communication between hearing and hard-of-hearing communities still relies heavily on trained interpreters or proficient individuals which often exacerbates their social exclusion, creating barriers in everyday interactions.

As an attempt to mitigate the barrier between sign and spoken languages, several methods have been developed to tackle sign language processing by unfolding the task into two research problems: sign language translation (SLT, sign-to-text) [

[6](#bib.bib6) , [84](#bib.bib84) , [24](#bib.bib24) , [77](#bib.bib77) , [64](#bib.bib64) ] and sign language generation (SLG, text-to-sign) [ [50](#bib.bib50) , [3](#bib.bib3) , [69](#bib.bib69) , [83](#bib.bib83) , [57](#bib.bib57) ] .

Numerous research efforts have established significant progress in SLT by leveraging 2D and 3D representations coupled with large-scale pretrained language models (LMs) [

[6](#bib.bib6) , [13](#bib.bib13) , [49](#bib.bib49) , [24](#bib.bib24) , [64](#bib.bib64) , [71](#bib.bib71) , [17](#bib.bib17) ] .

Despite the advent of SLT methods, the task of SLG remains relatively unexplored.

Current methods approach SLG as a visual content generation task, leveraging GANs [

[57](#bib.bib57) , [54](#bib.bib54) ] or diffusion models [ [3](#bib.bib3) , [43](#bib.bib43) ] thereby neglecting the linguistic nature of sign languages, leading to suboptimal performance and missing benefits of pretrained LMs such as generalizability and scalability [ [60](#bib.bib60) , [26](#bib.bib26) , [16](#bib.bib16) ] .

In particular, sign languages share the fundamental linguistic properties and the discrete structure of natural spoken languages [

[68](#bib.bib68) , [13](#bib.bib13) ] , which necessitates processing using language modeling techniques, rather than treating them as a continuous motion generation task.

A common approach to discretize sign languages is employing glosses-the written form of signs-as an intermediate representation [

[83](#bib.bib83) , [54](#bib.bib54) , [20](#bib.bib20) , [59](#bib.bib59) ] .

While glosses provide a step towards more natural modeling of sign languages, they require meticulous annotation efforts and impose a predefined information bottleneck that may fall short of fully capturing sign semantics.

In this work, we approach SLG using a discrete sign space to effectively capture the linguistic structure of sign languages.

To do so, we build a sign tokenizer that learns a mapping between continuous motions and discretized tokens using a compact quantized latent space.

With such formulation, we can frame the generative task in an autoregressive manner, akin to how natural languages are processed.

In this work, we introduce SOKE, a multilingual sign language generation model designed to produce 3D signs in an autoregressive manner. Unlike body motion modeling, sign languages utilize both manual and non-manual parameters to convey information [ [29](#bib.bib29) , [78](#bib.bib78) , [81](#bib.bib81) , [23](#bib.bib23) ] . Previous works [ [69](#bib.bib69) , [67](#bib.bib67) , [3](#bib.bib3) ] usually overlook the multi-cue nature of sign languages, treating SLG as a whole-body 3D motion generation task. In contrast, we propose a decoupled sign tokenizer that models hand articulations and body posture using distinct tokens. This approach enhances 3D sign modeling and effectively improves the generalization of the sign-token mapping.

Given the limited vocabulary size in continuous sign language datasets, we leverage a large-scale pretrained multilingual LM [

[32](#bib.bib32) ] to enhance generalization to an unconstrained domain of discourse. Additionally, to support multilingual SLG in a unified way, we fine-tune the proposed model on a multilingual sign language dataset that includes American and Chinese sign languages by incorporating a series of special tokens to specify the target sign language and body part.

To sum up, the contributions of this paper can be summarized as:

- • We propose Signs as Tokens (SOKE), a novel approach for sign language generation. With the aid of a pretrained LM, SOKE can deal with multiple sign languages in a unified model.
- • Motivated by the multi-cue property of sign languages, we propose a decoupled sign motion tokenizer (DETO) that can effectively capture semantics conveyed by various body parts.
- • We curate a large-scale Chinese sign language dataset, CSL-Daily [ [79](#bib.bib79) ] , with accurate SMPL-X [ [40](#bib.bib40) ] pose annotations.
- • We demonstrate that SOKE achieves state-of-the-art performance on two challenging benchmarks, How2Sign [ [8](#bib.bib8) ] and CSL-Daily [ [79](#bib.bib79) ] , using a single unified model.

## 2 Related Work

Sign Language Processing. There are various research topics in sign language processing, including sign language recognition (SLR) [ [36](#bib.bib36) , [27](#bib.bib27) , [18](#bib.bib18) , [17](#bib.bib17) , [82](#bib.bib82) , [12](#bib.bib12) ] , sign language translation (SLT) [ [5](#bib.bib5) , [28](#bib.bib28) , [6](#bib.bib6) , [66](#bib.bib66) , [13](#bib.bib13) , [72](#bib.bib72) ] , and sign language generation (SLG, also known as sign language production) [ [9](#bib.bib9) , [53](#bib.bib53) , [54](#bib.bib54) , [83](#bib.bib83) , [69](#bib.bib69) , [2](#bib.bib2) ] . Among these, SLT and SLG are two key tasks that focus on sign-to-text and text-to-sign conversion, respectively, constituting a two-way communication system between the deaf and hearing communities. Motivated by the linguistic nature of sign languages, a significant number of works [ [6](#bib.bib6) , [64](#bib.bib64) , [24](#bib.bib24) ] have integrated LMs pretrained on large natural language corpora into SLT pipelines and observed significant performance improvements.

In contrast with SLT, SLG remains largely unexplored. Gloss-based methods [ [83](#bib.bib83) , [54](#bib.bib54) , [70](#bib.bib70) , [65](#bib.bib65) , [19](#bib.bib19) , [59](#bib.bib59) , [20](#bib.bib20) ] approach SLG by utilizing glosses as intermediate representations, following a text-to-gloss-to-sign pipeline. However, glosses act as an information bottleneck, which limits the generation performance.

Several works [

[2](#bib.bib2) , [50](#bib.bib50) , [52](#bib.bib52) ] propose to directly regress 2D joints to represent sign poses, simplifying the modeling process, however, they suffer from mode collapse due to the limited scale of data.

Recent state-of-the-art SLG works can be categorized into two classes: the first group of methods [

[3](#bib.bib3) , [9](#bib.bib9) ] employ diffusion models to generate sign motions conditioned on text inputs; the second group of methods [ [7](#bib.bib7) , [69](#bib.bib69) , [67](#bib.bib67) ] consider the linguistic nature of sign languages and adopts a tokenizer-LM two-stage autoregressive generation approach.

Nevertheless, few works consider the multi-cue property of sign languages which limits their expressivity. In addition, there has been a notable gap in the literature on unified models for text-driven multilingual SLG.

In this work, we propose a decoupled tokenizer that maps the body and both hands into separated quantized latent spaces and address multilingual SLG in a unified model with pure text inputs.

Sign Tokenizers. Besides using glosses as discrete sign representations [ [83](#bib.bib83) , [70](#bib.bib70) ] , several studies [ [69](#bib.bib69) , [67](#bib.bib67) , [13](#bib.bib13) , [35](#bib.bib35) ] focus on training sign tokenizers using purely visual data. For instance, SignVAE [ [69](#bib.bib69) ] employs a basic VQ-VAE [ [61](#bib.bib61) , [15](#bib.bib15) ] to tokenize sign motions, while DVQ-VAE [ [67](#bib.bib67) ] introduces a dynamic quantizing technique to adjust encoding length. In the field of SLT, VQ-Sign [ [13](#bib.bib13) ] tokenizes sign videos at both the character and word levels.

In contrast to the previous methods, we propose a simple yet effective decoupled tokenizer, which is deeply embedded with the task of SLG, considering the multi-cue nature of sign languages.

Motion Generation. Motion generation is an important area of research with substantial impacts on domains such as human-computer interaction and robotics. Early studies [ [14](#bib.bib14) , [41](#bib.bib41) ] focus on generating motions based on predefined action categories, which constrain their practical applications. With the emergence of vision-language models [ [44](#bib.bib44) ] , recent motion generation methods have shifted to using text as a condition, making them more accessible and intuitive. These methods can be categorized into two main types: 1) Diffusion-based approaches [ [75](#bib.bib75) , [56](#bib.bib56) , [4](#bib.bib4) ] , which utilize latent diffusion [ [47](#bib.bib47) ] to generate motions from Gaussian noises, incorporating text conditions through a cross-attention mechanism. 2) Autoregressive approaches [ [22](#bib.bib22) , [76](#bib.bib76) , [80](#bib.bib80) , [74](#bib.bib74) , [15](#bib.bib15) ] , which draw inspiration from text generation methods by first tokenizing motion sequences and then using language models to perform autoregressive generation.

Applying motion generation methods in the domain of SLG has demonstrated particularly promising results [ [3](#bib.bib3) , [67](#bib.bib67) ] . However, a major limitation of these studies is their insufficient attention to hand articulations. In this work, we propose a sign-language-specific tokenizer to emphasize on complex hand articulations.

## 3 Methodology

### 3.1 Data Preparation

Although NSA [ [3](#bib.bib3) ] and SignAvatars [ [69](#bib.bib69) ] have made efforts in curating high-quality (pseudo) SMPL-X annotations for an American Sign Language (ASL) dataset, How2Sign [ [8](#bib.bib8) ] , there is a notable lack of 3D annotations for other continuous sign language datasets, impeding multilingual SLG research.

In this work, we rely on a two-stage approach, leveraging state-of-the-art 3D pose estimation methods [

[31](#bib.bib31) , [42](#bib.bib42) ] , to extract high-fidelity SMPL-X poses for CSL-Daily [ [79](#bib.bib79) ] , the largest Chinese Sign Language (CSL) dataset.

Specifically, we initially estimate a set of rough 3D body poses for each signer using OSX [

[31](#bib.bib31) ] along with accurate 3D hand poses from WiLoR [ [42](#bib.bib42) ] .

Given that OSX often falls short in accurately capturing the arm poses, we follow [

[3](#bib.bib3) ] and refine the upper body joint rotations by minimizing a re-projection loss between the estimated joints and the detected 2D joint keypoints from Mediapipe [ [34](#bib.bib34) ] .

Using this process we can ensure accurate whole-body annotations.

We represent each sign motion sequence as 𝐒 ∈ ℝ T × d 𝐒 superscript ℝ 𝑇 𝑑 \mathbf{S}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times d} , where T 𝑇 T is the sequence length and d = 133 𝑑 133 d=133 denotes the number of SMPL-X parameters, including 11 upper body joints, 30 hand joints, and 10 expression parameters. For additional details, we refer the reader to the supplementary materials.

### 3.3 Decoupled Tokenizer

To discretize continuous signs into tokens, we develop a sign tokenizer based on the well-established VQ-VAE [ [61](#bib.bib61) , [22](#bib.bib22) ] . Existing motion generation research primarily focuses on body movements, such as running and jumping, while often neglecting the role of hands. However, in sign language, hands are crucial for conveying information [ [17](#bib.bib17) , [55](#bib.bib55) ] .

To bridge this gap, we propose DETO, a decoupled tokenizer that utilizes three VQ-VAEs to simultaneously model key regions: the upper body and both hands. By assigning two of the VQ-VAEs specifically to the hands, we can independently model the hand motions and effectively improve the expressivity of the model.

## 4 Experiments

Datasets. We construct a multilingual sign language dataset by merging two widely adopted datasets: How2Sign [ [8](#bib.bib8) ] and CSL-Daily [ [79](#bib.bib79) ] . How2Sign is a large-scale American sign language dataset, consisting of 31K, 1.7K, and 2.3K video-text pairs in training, dev, and test sets, respectively. CSL-Daily is the largest existing continuous Chinese sign language dataset, containing 18K samples for training, 1K for development, and 1.1K for testing.

To train SOKE, we use the SMPL-X annotations of How2Sign dataset as provided in [

[3](#bib.bib3) ] along with the curated SMPL-X annotations of CSL-Daily as discussed in Section [3.1](#S3.SS1) .

Evaluation Metrics. To quantitatively assess the reconstruction quality of our proposed DETO, we use Procrustes-aligned mean per vertex position error (PA-MPVPE) and mean per joint position error (PA-MPJPE) as described in [ [31](#bib.bib31) , [69](#bib.bib69) ] . Additionally, since the length of generated signs may differ from the ground truth, we employ the well-established dynamic time warping (DTW) [ [37](#bib.bib37) ] on joint errors, which calculates sequence-level distances between the generated signs and ground truth. Furthermore, in line with [ [3](#bib.bib3) ] , we also report back-translation scores, including BLEU-4 [ [39](#bib.bib39) ] and ROUGE [ [30](#bib.bib30) ] , to assess the interpretability of the generated signs.

### 4.1 Qualitative Evaluation

Reconstructed Signs. As depicted in Figure [5](#S4.F5) , we investigate the impact of decoupled tokenizer (DETO) on sign reconstruction. The visualizations reveal that substituting DETO with a single VQ-VAE for full-body motion reconstruction leads to diminished quality in hand movements, especially when the signer is performing complex handshapes (the first and second columns of Figure [5](#S4.F5) ).

Generated Signs. We first reimplement a state-of-the-art (SOTA) motion generation approach, MotionGPT [ [22](#bib.bib22) ] , which utilizes a single VQ-VAE (MotionVAE) to discretize whole-body motions and employs a monolingual language model [ [45](#bib.bib45) ] , on sign language datasets. In Figure [6](#S4.F6) , we perform a qualitative comparison between our proposed method, SOKE, and MotionGPT. As can be easily seen, our proposed method produces significantly better results, with visual quality comparable to the ground truth sign videos in both the ASL and CSL datasets. Notably, our method can generate precise and informative hand movements, whereas MotionGPT struggles with poor hand motion generation, suggesting the effectiveness of decoupled tokenizing for hands. Additionally, the proposed method supports both sign languages simultaneously, whereas MotionGPT is monolingual and requires separate models for each sign language dataset.

### 4.2 Quantitative Evaluation

Comparison with SOTA SLG Methods. In addition to the above qualitative comparison, we also perform a quantitative evaluation against SOTA SLG methods, as detailed in Table [1](#S4.T1) .

Text2Mesh [

[58](#bib.bib58) ] is a pioneering work in generating 3D sign motions from texts. However, the model depends on glosses as an intermediate supervision to learn sign-text mappings. This reliance limits its ability to generalize to large-scale datasets that lack gloss annotations. Additionally, glosses have inherent limitations, such as inadequate representation [ [38](#bib.bib38) ] , which can cause significant error propagation, resulting in high DTW errors (&gt;11 for both hands).

Neural Sign Actors (NSA) [

[3](#bib.bib3) ] currently represents the leading SLG approach, framing SLG as a motion generation task and utilizing a diffusion model as its backbone. However, NSA is a monolingual generator, tested solely on How2Sign, which raises concerns about its ability to generalize across different sign languages. In comparison, our method achieves a similar DTW error for body and significantly lower errors for both hands, demonstrating the effectiveness of our decoupled tokenizer. Furthermore, with just a single unified model, our approach achieves very low hand errors of 2.20/2.66 on CSL-Daily.

Comparison with SOTA Sign Motion Tokenizers. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our decoupled tokenizer (DETO), we reimplement two SOTA sign motion tokenizers: SignVAE [ [69](#bib.bib69) ] and MotionVAE [ [22](#bib.bib22) ] . Both approaches employ a single VQ-VAE to tokenize full-body motions, while MotionVAE incorporates advanced training techniques such as EMA reset [ [46](#bib.bib46) ] to enhance codebook utilization. As illustrated in Table [2](#S4.T2) , our DETO significantly surpasses both methods across all metrics, including reconstruction errors for various body parts, by a substantial margin. These results strongly indicate the benefits of decoupled modeling for sign languages. Additionally, Table [2](#S4.T2) reveals that tokenizer training benefits from multilingual data; all methods show consistent performance improvements when trained on combined datasets compared to training on each dataset individually. This is consistent with the finding in [ [62](#bib.bib62) ] , in which the authors leverage cross-lingual signs to boost sign language recognition.

Ablation Study on Decoupled Tokenizer. Inspired by the multi-cue characteristics of sign languages, we introduce a decoupled tokenizer that discretizes sign motion sequences for different body parts. In Table [3](#S4.T3) , we present an ablation study on various configurations: a single VQ-VAE for whole-body motions, one VAE for the body and another for both hands (Body+Hands), and an additional VAE for facial expressions. The findings indicate that progressively separating the body and hands leads to significantly improved results, particularly benefiting hand reconstruction, with hand joint errors decreasing from 7.82 to 6.65 on How2Sign and from 5.99 to 5.20 on CSL-Daily. However, we did not observe clear performance improvements when adding a VAE for facial expressions, likely due to the low dimensionality of facial expression parameters, which are limited to 10 dimensions. In the future, we aim to employ more advanced facial expression models to annotate detailed expression parameters for sign language datasets.

Impact of Tokenizer and Multilingual Data on SLG. In Table [4](#S4.T4) , we examine the effects of using a decoupled tokenizer and multilingual data on the overall generation performance. The results suggest two key insights. First, DETO is crucial for achieving high-quality generation performance, as its removal results in an increase of 1.36 in hand DTW errors on CSL-Daily, underscoring the effectiveness of decoupled modeling in sign language generation. Second, incorporating multilingual data enhances generation performance, primarily due to the development of a more robust tokenizer, as highlighted in Table [2](#S4.T2) .

### 4.3 User Study

The ultimate goal of sign language generation research is to bridge the communication gap between deaf and hearing individuals. In addition to the objective metrics presented in the previous tables, we conducted a user study involving five professional ASL signers. These signers were asked to rate the alignment between generated signs and text annotations on a scale from 1 to 10, with higher scores indicating better semantic conveyance of the texts. Ground truth sign motions were also included in the study. Specifically, we provided 15 generated signs from both the baseline, MotionGPT, and our proposed method, SOKE. The order of the signs was randomly shuffled to prevent potential bias.

As shown in Figure [7](#S4.F7) , our method achieved an encouraging average rating of 5.24, significantly surpassing the baseline, which received a low score of 1.36.

These results align with the above qualitative and quantitative evaluations, indicating the potential of our method in constituting a two-way communication system between the deaf and hearing.

## 5 Conclusion

In this paper, we introduce SOKE, an autoregressive multilingual sign language generator. Unlike existing approaches that treat sign language generation as a visual content generation task, we focus on the linguistic nature of sign languages, characterized by discrete semantic units. To this end, we develop a specialized sign motion tokenizer that maps continuous motions to discretized tokens in a latent space. Additionally, inspired by the multi-channel characteristics of sign languages, we decompose the tokenizer into three parallel components to independently model different body parts. Once sign motions are discretized, we utilize a pretrained multilingual LM to perform autoregressive generation using a set of customized special tokens. Furthermore, we curate CSL-Daily with 3D pose annotations to support multilingual SLG. Extensive qualitative and quantitative evaluations on a combined ASL-CSL dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method.

## Appendix D Broader Impacts and Limitations

Broader Impacts. Sign language is the primary mode of communication for the deaf communities. Due to significant grammatical differences from spoken languages, a notable communication gap exists between the deaf and hearing individuals. In this work, we propose an autoregressive sign language model, which is capable of generating multilingual sign language avatars from text inputs within a single unified framework. Extensive quantitative and qualitative results suggest the potential of our method to form a practical deaf-hearing communication system.

Limitations. Our method employs 3D avatars to represent signers, enabling high-fidelity motion representations. However, there is a lack of 3D annotations in existing sign language datasets. While our proposed SMPL-X pose fitting pipeline can accurately reconstruct 3D meshes from 2D keypoints, some reconstruction errors are inevitable. In the future, the release of more sign language datasets with annotated meshes is anticipated, which could significantly enhance avatar-based sign language generation models.

We have validated the proposed multilingual sign language generator on two widely-adopted sign languages: Chinese Sign Language, which is used by approximately 4 million people in China, and American Sign Language, with around half a million users in North America [ [63](#bib.bib63) ] . In the future, we plan to extend our method to support additional sign languages, such as British Sign Language [ [1](#bib.bib1) ] and Indian Sign Language [ [25](#bib.bib25) ] .
