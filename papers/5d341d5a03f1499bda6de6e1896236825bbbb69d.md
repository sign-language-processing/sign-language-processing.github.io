## Abstract

Automatic Sign Language Translation (SLT) has gained increasing attention as a means to bridge communication barriers between deaf and hearing communities. Early systems primarily relied on recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) architectures, which demonstrated promise on short sequences but struggled with long-distance dependencies and real-time deployment. The advent of transformer architectures and large language models (LLMs) has revolutionized the field by enabling gloss-free translation, improving grammaticality, and producing scalable multilingual systems. This paper surveys recent advances in SLT, analyzing both gloss-based and gloss-free paradigms, reviewing benchmark datasets, and comparing innovative architectures, including adaptive transformers, pseudo-gloss generation pipelines, and LLM-integrated frameworks. We highlight key challenges, including dataset scarcity, real-time translation efficiency, and the integration of non-manual linguistic features. We will then outline future directions toward robust, real-time, and universally deployable SLT systems.
