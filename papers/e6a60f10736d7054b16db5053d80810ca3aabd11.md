## Abstract

Despite progress in gloss-free Sign Language Translation (SLT), traditional single modality end-to-end approaches consistently fail on two critical components of natural signing: the precise recognition of high-speed fingerspelling and the integration of asynchronous non-manual cues from the face. Recent progress in SLT with Large Language Models has side stepped this challenge, forcing a single network to learn these simultaneously resulting in poor performance when tasked with translating crucial information such as names, places, and technical terms. We introduce SignBind-LLM, a modular framework designed to overcome these limitations. Our approach employs separate, specialized predictors for continuous signing, fingerspelling, and lipreading. Each expert network first decodes its specific modality into a sequence of tokens. These parallel streams are then fused by a lightweight transformer that resolves temporal misalignments before passing the combined representation to a Large Language Model (LLM) for final sentence generation. Our method establishes a new state-of-the-art on the How2Sign, ChicagoFSWildPlus, and BOBSL datasets with a BLEU-4 score of 22.1, 73.2% letter accuracy and BLEU-4 score of 6.8 respectively. These results validate our core hypothesis: isolating and solving distinct recognition tasks before fusion provides a more powerful and effective pathway to robust, high-fidelity sign language translation.

# MultiStream-LLM: Bridging Modalities for Robust Sign Language Translation

Marshall Thomas,

Edward Fish,

Richard Bowden

###### Abstract

Despite progress in gloss-free Sign Language Translation (SLT), monolithic end-to-end models consistently fail on two critical components of natural signing: the precise recognition of high-speed fingerspelling and the integration of asynchronous non-manual cues from the face. Recent progress in Automated Sign Language Translation with Large Language Models has side stepped this challenge, forcing a single network to learn these simultaneously resulting in poor performance when tasked with translating crucial information such as names,places, and technical terms.

We introduce MultiStream-LLM , a modular framework designed to overcome these limitations. Our approach employs separate, specialized predictors for continuous signing, fingerspelling, and lipreading. Each expert network first decodes its specific modality into a sequence of tokens. These parallel streams are then fused by a lightweight transformer that resolves temporal misalignments before passing the combined representation to a Large Language Model (LLM) for final sentence generation.

Our method establishes a new state-of-the-art on the How2Sign benchmark with a BLEU-4 score of 23.5 and achieves 73.2% letter accuracy on the challenging ChicagoFSWildPlus fingerspelling dataset. These results validate our core hypothesis: by isolating and solving distinct recognition tasks before fusion, our multi-expert approach provides a more powerful and effective pathway to robust, high-fidelity sign language translation.

## Introduction

Sign languages are fully fledged natural languages that are used by millions of Deaf and hard-of-hearing people around the world to communicate. They make use of both manual gestures (handshapes, movements and locations) alongside non-manual signals (facial expressions, mouthings, and mouth gestures 1 1 1 In sign language linguistics, *mouthing* denotes silent articulation of spoken words that accompany signs, while *mouth gestures* are nonverbal movements integral to the sign itself, conveying grammatical or affective nuance ). They vary from country to country, for example: American Sign Language (ASL), British Sign Language (BSL) and German Sign Language (DGS) (HandSpeak [2024](#bib.bib24) ; British Sign Language [2024](#bib.bib4) ; GebärdenVerstehen [2024](#bib.bib19) ) where each possesses its own lexicon and grammar, even if many share spatial and syntactic parallels.

Early methods focused on Isolated Sign Language Recognition (ISLR) but relied on expertly annotated datasets. These annotations include "glosses (Camgoz et al. [2018](#bib.bib5) ) " which are English translations for individual words. More recent works have demonstrated that glosses are no longer a strict requirement for effective SLT with the introduction of large, gloss-free datasets (e.g. YouTube-ASL (Uthus, Tanzer, and Georg [2023](#bib.bib54) ) , Public DGS (Hanke et al. [2020](#bib.bib25) ) , How2Sign (Duarte et al. [2021](#bib.bib11) ) ) as well as increased model size and the use of LLM's (Lin et al. [2023](#bib.bib33) ) . However even with ChicagoFSWildPlus (B. Shi and Livescu [2019](#bib.bib3) ) a dataset focused on ASL fingerspelling modern approaches often struggle with two key challenges:

- • Fingerspelling: Used for proper names, technical terms and loanwords' fingerspelling makes up roughly 12-35% of ASL, 8-20% of BSL, and 5-10% of DGS (Valli et al. [2011](#bib.bib55) ; Pfau, Steinbach, and Woll [2012](#bib.bib41) ) . Despite its importance, it remains under-supported in gloss-free SLT systems like Llava-SLT (Liang et al. [2024](#bib.bib32) ) where it is often treated as just another sub-task of the full translation pipeline, which leads to:
- • Non-manual cues Mouthings and facial expressions carry critical disambiguating signals, akin to phonemes or visemes in speech (Encyclopædia Britannica [2024](#bib.bib12) ; Ozel [2024](#bib.bib37) ) but existing SLT models rarely exploit them, mostly due to the asynchronous nature of these signals.

In parallel, breakthroughs in Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) (Long et al. [2025](#bib.bib34) ) and Visual Speech Recognition (VSR, i.e., lipreading) (Thomas, Fish, and Bowden [2025](#bib.bib52) ) demonstrate how large scale pretraining and powerful architectures such as ViTs (Dosovitskiy et al. [2021](#bib.bib10) ) and LLM decoders (Radford et al. [2022](#bib.bib42) ) can deliver human-level performance on spoken language tasks. Yet sign language systems lag behind, largely because (a) large, freely available signed corpora are scarce; (b) the spatiotemporal complexity of co-articulated gestures exceeds that of spoken-language signals; and (c) unlike speech, signing and mouthing are often *offset* in time especially during fingerspelling, where manual letters may precede or follow their mouthings.

To address these gaps, we introduce MultiStream-LLM , a *gloss-free* SLT framework that:

1. 1. Integrates three modalities continuous sign recognition, fingerspelling detection, and lipreading.
2. 2. Fuses their complementary signals via a lightweight transformer encoder that explicitly handles temporal asynchronicity.
3. 3. Decodes the fused representation with a fine-tuned Large Language Model, yielding contextually accurate translations without any direct gloss supervision.

We evaluate MultiStream-LLM on YouTube-ASL and ChicagoFSWildPlus, achieving state-of-the-art results: sequence classification accuracy of 99.2%, letter accuracy of 73.2%, and BLEU-4 of 23.5. Our approach demonstrates that (1) non-manual cues especially lipreading-significantly boost gloss-free SLT, and (2) combining modality-specific predictors with an LLM yields scalable, high-quality translation for real-world signed content.

## Related Work

### Sign Language Understanding

Sign Language Understanding (SLU) has evolved through three increasingly ambitious tasks:

1. 1. Isolated Sign Language Recognition (ISLR): Early work focused on recognizing individual signs in controlled settings. Methods such as 3D CNNs over spatiotemporal volumes (Min et al. [2021](#bib.bib36) ) and hybrid CNN-HMM pipelines (Koller et al. [2019](#bib.bib28) ) achieved high accuracy on small vocabularies (e.g., 100-500 signs), but relied on carefully segmented clips and extensive manual gloss annotations.
2. 2. Continuous Sign Language Recognition (CSLR): To scale beyond isolated signs, CSLR methods adopted sequence-level training with Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) loss (Graves et al. [2006](#bib.bib21) ) . Recurrent architectures (CNN-BiLSTM-CTC) (Graves and Schmidhuber [2005](#bib.bib22) ) and, more recently, Transformer encoders (Min et al. [2021](#bib.bib36) ) enabled end-to-end gloss prediction from unsegmented video. While these models tolerate variable sign durations, they still depend on gloss-level transcripts and struggle with out-of-vocabulary signs and fingerspelling.
3. 3. Sign Language Translation (SLT): SLT goes one step further by generating natural language sentences directly from sign video.

### Lipreading and Visual Speech Recognition

Lipreading, or visual speech recognition (VSR), can complement sign language understanding by capturing silent speech cues. Modern systems employ deep learning-based architectures. LipNet (Assael et al. [2016](#bib.bib2) ) introduced an end-to-end model using spatiotemporal CNNs (Lecun et al. [1998](#bib.bib29) ; Carreira and Zisserman [2018](#bib.bib7) ) and BiLSTMs (Graves and Schmidhuber [2005](#bib.bib22) ) , achieving state-of-the-art sentence-level lipreading performance. Transformer-based models, such as Lipformer (Xue et al. [2023](#bib.bib58) ) , further improved contextual understanding across video frames.

Recent advances leverage self-supervised learning and multimodal fusion to enhance lipreading robustness. AV-HuBERT (Shi et al. [2022b](#bib.bib47) ) demonstrated the efficacy of large-scale audiovisual pretraining, achieving strong performance under challenging conditions (Thomas, Fish, and Bowden [2025](#bib.bib52) ) . However, lipreading remains underexplored in the context of fingerspelling and full-sentence SLT, dispite mouthing cues carrying up to 40% of linguistic information in natural sign production (e.g., lexical disambiguation, grammatical markers) (Aparicio et al. [2017](#bib.bib1) ) .

### Multimodal Approaches

Multimodal learning has emerged as a promising approach to improve fingerspelling detection by integrating visual, linguistic, and contextual information. Previous systems fused RGB video, pose sequences, and optical flow to enhance recognition accuracy. Recent works such as GFSLT-VLP (Zhou et al. [2023](#bib.bib61) ) incorporated video-text contrastive learning and cross-modal attention mechanisms to bridge the gap between sign language and natural language representations.

However, current fingerspelling detection methods remain focused on hand centric cues and fail to leverage the rich information present in lip movements. Approaches such as synthetic data augmentation (Fowley and Ventresque [2022](#bib.bib15) ) , large "in-the-wild" collections (Zhidebayeva et al. [2024](#bib.bib60) ; Georg et al. [2024](#bib.bib20) ) , and pose-based models achieve moderate accuracy on isolated letter streams but do not integrate mouthings. This design overlooks the well-documented co-articulation between handshapes and lip movements in natural signing (Aparicio et al. [2017](#bib.bib1) ) , leading to persistent errors on homonymous handshapes (e.g., "M" vs. "N") and proper-noun detection. To date, no SLT system has explicitly fused high-resolution fingerspelling and lipreading streams while accounting for their temporal misalignment.

### LLMs in SLT and VSR

The rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) has revolutionized sign language understanding, fingerspelling detection, and lipreading by leveraging vast linguistic knowledge and multimodal learning capabilities. In SLT, LLMs have been employed to bridge the gap between sign video sequences and natural language text. Newer approaches (Ruiz and Martinez [2025](#bib.bib43) ) applied transformer-based architectures to generate coherent text from sign videos, outperforming traditional gloss-based approaches. GFSLT-VLP (Zhou et al. [2023](#bib.bib61) ) further enhanced gloss-free SLT performance.

For fingerspelling detection, LLMs have facilitated cross-modal learning by aligning hand gestures with corresponding textual representations (Tanzer [2024](#bib.bib50) ; Shi [2023](#bib.bib45) ) . These approaches demonstrated that LLMs can effectively enhance fingerspelling recognition by integrating linguistic and visual modalities.

In lipreading, LLMs have been integrated into audiovisual speech recognition pipelines to improve contextual understanding and transcription accuracy. The first approach to utilize LLM-based encoders (Yeo et al. [2024](#bib.bib59) ) enhanced lipreading robustness, even under challenging conditions. These advancements underscore the potential of LLMs to bridge modality gaps and improve performance across SLT, fingerspelling, and lipreading tasks.

### Pre-Processing Pipeline

In the pre-processing stage, the input videos are segmented into two distinct video sequences using MediaPipe (Lugaresi et al. [2019](#bib.bib35) ) . The first sequence consists of cropped frames that contain just the hands, X h  a  n  d  s X\_{hands} . The second sequence consists of cropped frames that contain just the face, X f  a  c  e X\_{face} . Both video sequences are resized to a consistent resolution of 224 × 224 224\times 224 pixels per frame. Each video is normalized and converted to batches of shape B × T × 3 × 224 × 224 B\times T\times 3\times 224\times 224 , where B B is the batch size, T T is the number of frames, and the last three dimensions represent the RGB channels, height, and width giving:

|    | X  ∈  ℝ  B  ×  T  ×  3  ×  224  ×  224  X\in\mathbb{R}^{B\times T\times 3\times 224\times 224}   |    | (2)   |
|----|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----|-------|

As well as pre-processing the video inputs we also pre-process the corresponding labels. This entails Pseudo-Glossing to remove unneeded text from labels to reduce the required lexicon of the Sign Language Prediction model.

Based on the predicted sequence type, the system dynamically selects the appropriate network to process either the fingerspelling or continuos sign alongside the lip reading from the video input X X . We define three additional 'expert' networks that can be selected to extract these features

Simultaneously, we use a Lip Reading model which is comprised of a masked feature extractor, an adapter network and a CTC head. Given a batch of face-cropped video frames X X we first apply random patch masking (50% drop rate) and add learnable positional embeddings. These masked frames are then encoded by a ViT to produce per-frame feature vectors Z Z . Next we pass Z Z through an adapter network consisting of a cascade of 1D convolutional layers and a pooling layer to downsample the temporal dimension while retaining an intermediate feature dimension D a  d  a  p  t  e  r D\_{adapter} . Finally, a CTC head maps these downsampled features to phonemes L L .

### Multimodal Feature Fusion

To integrate information from manual (signs, fingerspelling) and non-manual (lips) modalities while addressing their temporal asynchronicity, we employ a transformer-based fusion module.

First, we project the token-level embeddings for each modality into a shared fusion dimension D D :

|    | L  sign  ′  \displaystyle L^{\prime}\_{\mathrm{sign}}   | =  L  sign  W  sign  +  b  sign  ,  \displaystyle=L\_{\mathrm{sign}}W\_{\mathrm{sign}}+b\_{\mathrm{sign}},   |    | (8)   |
|----|---------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----|-------|
|    | L  fs  ′  \displaystyle L^{\prime}\_{\mathrm{fs}}       | =  L  fs  W  fs  +  b  fs  ,  \displaystyle=L\_{\mathrm{fs}}W\_{\mathrm{fs}}+b\_{\mathrm{fs}},               |    | (8)   |

This combination is weighted by Gumbel-Softmax scores ( g sign , g fs g\_{\mathrm{sign}},g\_{\mathrm{fs}} ) from a sequence-type classifier that determines the active modality for each token. where σ \sigma is the sigmoid function. The final fused representation, H fused H\_{\mathrm{fused}} , is produced by:

|    | H  fused  =  G  ⊙  M  +  (  1  −  G  )  ⊙  L  lip  ′  H\_{\mathrm{fused}}=G\odot M+(1-G)\odot L^{\prime}\_{\mathrm{lip}}   |    | (11)   |

where ⊙ \odot denotes element-wise multiplication.

### Training and Optimization

Rather than traditional end-to-end training, we employ a staged training schedule in which each component is optimized separately. Firstly, the Sequence Type Classifier and all three Modality-Specific Predictors are pre-trained. Then all the pre-trained stages are recombined and frozen to train the Fusion Transformer. Finally, the LLM is fine-tuned to recreate sentences from the Fusion Transformer output tokens.

## Experiments

This section discusses the datasets used for training and evaluation, and the metrics employed to assess model performance.

### Datasets

We pre-trained our model using the Youtube-ASL dataset and ChicagoFSWild+, then fine-tuned it on How2Sign. We then evaluated our proposed framework on How2Sign and ChicagoFSWild+.

#### [YouTube-ASL (Uthus, Tanzer, and Georg 2023 ) :](#bib.bib54)

A large-scale ASL dataset comprising 60K videos and 1,000 hours sourced from YouTube, annotated with sentence-level transcriptions. This dataset provides diverse signing conditions, including variations in background, lighting, and signer appearance. The dataset features over 2,500 unique signers, ensuring that the model generalizes well across different users.

#### [ChicagoFSWildPlus (B. Shi and Livescu 2019 ) :](#bib.bib3)

A dataset containing 55,232 ASL finger-spelling sequences performed by 260 signers containing in-the-wild videos of fingerspelling sequences. It includes multiple signers, varied environments, and occlusions, which makes it ideal for evaluating the robustness of fingerspelling detection.

#### [How2Sign (Duarte et al. 2021 ) :](#bib.bib11)

How2Sign is a large-scale, continuous ASL corpus derived from 2,456 instructional "How2" videos, totaling over 8080 hours of footage. Recordings were made in two settings: a Green-Screen studio (79.1 h over 2,529 videos) and the Panoptic studio (2.96 h over 124 videos). Eleven signers (5 hearing ASL interpreters, 2 hard-of-hearing, 4 Deaf) produced 35,19135,191 sentence-level clips (average 162 frames/5.4 s, 17 words), yielding a vocabulary of over 16,000 English words.

### Evaluation Metrics

We evaluated model performance using the following metrics:

#### [Letter Accuracy (Garofolo et al. 1993 ) :](#bib.bib18)

Evaluates performance at the character level, particularly important for fingerspelling. It captures errors in individual letter predictions.

#### Sequence Accuracy:

The percentage of correctly predicted sequences. This metric reflects the model's ability to classify the type of sequence, be it Sign, Fingerspelling or Nothing.

#### [BLEU Score (Papineni et al. 2002 ) :](#bib.bib40)

Assesses the quality of generated sequences by comparing them to reference translations. A higher BLEU score indicates better alignment with human-generated annotations.

#### [ROUGE (Ganesan 2018 ) :](#bib.bib17)

Measures the overlap of n-grams, longest common subsequences, and word sequences between a generated summary (or translation) and one or more reference texts, prioritizing recall of relevant content.

### Ablation Study

In this section, we discuss the different ablation studies performed to demonstrate the contribution of the various components in the architecture.

#### Model Variants:

The first ablation was to understand the contribution of each modality and component, we conducted an ablation study by selectively disabling key features of MultiStream-LLM. Table [3](#Sx5.T3) presents the results.

The ablation results highlight the importance of each component. Removal of the lip-reading model leads to a decrease in Letter Accuracy (18.9%) and a decrease in the BLEU score (6.9), indicating that lip cues play a crucial role in disambiguating finger-spelled words and an important role in clarifying Sign Language. Removing the LLM and leaving the CTC head results in the largest performance drop, 14.4% for Letter Accuracy and 15.7 for BLEU-4, showing the importance the LLM has in sentence reconstruction.

#### Varying LLMs:

The next ablation was to compare the effectiveness of different LLMs, fully fine-tuned with different parameter sizes. Table [4](#Sx5.T4) shows the results of this study.

#### Glossing Methods:

This ablation compares different forms of pseudo-glossing to determine their effects on model performance, these include:

- • Random Glossing: N-number of words are randomly selected from the list of possible labels such that every label contains at least n of these Glosses
- • Probability Glossing: N-number of words are selected from the list of possible labels based on the frequency they show up (if two entries have the same frequency then the first label alphabetically is selected)
- • Pseudo-Glossing: N-number of words are selected based on their importance to sentence structure

The results of Table [5](#Sx5.T5) show that the optimum method of glossing is LLM glossing, where an LLM determines which tokens are the most crucial for sentence reconstruction. For example: the LLM dynamically deciding that in one sentence an auxiliary verb is crucial, whereas in another it might be safely dropped. Where as a static approach such as using WordNet is unable to do that. We can also see that not using a Glossing method still produces superior results to the limited Glossing methods. For example: Randomly Glossing 2 words in the label "I'm going to teach you how to make one of my favourite paper airplanes." would give insufficient information for the LLM to recreate the sentence.

#### Asynchronous Fusion:

The final ablation focuses on the effectiveness of the fusion model. The aim is to quantify how sensitive our fusion mechanism is to the temporal mis-alignment that naturally occurs between hand movements (manual signals) and mouth movements (non-manual cues) in real sign language. To quantify this we test with four different temporal shifts:

1. 1. No shift: Directly fuse frame t t of lips with frame t t of hands, no temporal shift at all
2. 2. Δ ± \Delta\pm 5 frames: fuse frame t of hands with lip t ± 3 t\pm 3 frames
3. 3. Δ ± \Delta\pm 10 frames: fuse frame t of hands with lip t ± 5 t\pm 5 frames
4. 4. Learned Alignment: The model learn an optimal per-time fusion gating

## Conclusion

In this paper, we introduced MultiStream-LLM , a novel framework for gloss-free sign language translation that integrates lip-reading, fingerspelling detection, and continuous sign language recognition into a unified pipeline. By leveraging a transformer-based architecture and an LLM-driven sequence prediction head, MultiStream-LLM demonstrated significant improvements over conventional CTC-based approaches. Our model effectively addresses the challenges posed by asynchronous modalities and enhances translation accuracy through multimodal fusion.
