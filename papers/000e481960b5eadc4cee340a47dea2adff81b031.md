## Abstract

Abstract This article looks at cross-linguistic variation in lexical iconicity, addressing the question of to what extent and how this variation is patterned. More than in spoken languages, iconicity is highly frequent in the lexicons of sign languages. It is also highly complex, in that often multiple motivated components jointly shape an iconic lexeme. Recent typological research on spoken languages finds tentative iconic patterning in a large number of basic lexical items, underlining once again the significance of iconicity for human language. The uncontested and widespread use of iconicity found in the lexicons of sign languages enables us to take typological research into lexical iconicity to the next level. Indeed, previous studies have shown cross-linguistic variation in: a) the use of embodying and handling handshapes in sign languages (mostly of European origin) and b) the frequency of space-based size depiction in African and European sign languages. The two types of variation may be interrelated, as handling handshapes may use space-based size depiction. In this study, we first replicate earlier studies on the distribution of embodying and handling handshapes, this time in a data set consisting of a relatively large set of sign languages (n = 11), most of which are used in Africa. The results confirm significant variation across these sign languages. These findings are then compared to the use of space-based size depiction, revealing that these patterns independently from the distribution of embodying/handling handshapes. We argue that the results call for expanding typological studies on representational strategies in iconic signs beyond the now relatively well studied instrument/manipulation alternation. Fine-grained analyses on a multitude of iconic features in signs are likely to reveal cross-linguistic variation in iconic tendencies in SL lexicons.

## 1 Introduction

Iconicity is a semiotic feature found both in spoken and signed languages, whereby a linguistic form resembles one or more aspects of its meaning (Peirce 1932). Taub (2001) presents her Analogue Building model for linguistic iconicity in spoken and signed languages, whereby a linguistic element is mapped onto a mental image. Following Peirce (1932) and Haiman (1985), Dingemanse (2011) distinguishes imagic iconicity as a sub-type of iconic form-meaning mappings. Imagic iconicity refers to the most direct type of mapping, for example particular speech sounds depicting a sound image in an iconic word in a spoken language, such as cuckoo depicting the sound made by the bird. Imagic or direct (Perniss et al. 2010) iconicity in sign languages (SLs) can consist of any visual language element, for example a particular con fi guration of the hand, movement, a location, or a facial expression, depicting a visual image. An example would be a sign for PREGNANT, whereby the hand traces the outline of a big belly. Imagic iconicity seems to be considerably more frequent in the lexicons of SLs than in those of spoken languages. Even in motivated words like ideophones, the proportion of imagic iconic items may be limited in spoken languages. 1 It is the visual-spatial nature of sign languages together with our dominant visual-spatial perception of the world around us seems a likely explanation for the ' abundance of direct iconic, visual-to-visual mappings ' in SLs (Perniss et al. 2010: 4). In fact, imagic iconicity is so abundant in part of the lexicons of sign languages that this seems to have kept sign languages from being considered full- fl edged languages for many decades (cf. Perniss et al. 2010).

Whatis it that determines the shape of iconic lexemes in human languages? As widely observed in both spoken and signed languages, there is considerable cross-linguistic variation in the actual forms of imagic iconic signs. Little is known about the principles governing this variation, or about the process underlying the creation of iconic signs more generally. Cross-linguistic studies in spoken languages suggest that there may be universal, iconic tendencies, such as the use of high front vowels in diminutives and high back vowels for augmentatives (Universal #1926, Plank and Filmoniva ' s Universals Archive, Konstanz). Other studies, however, do not agree that this is a universal tendency (e.g. GregovГЎ et al. 2010). Recent cross-linguistic studies on large sets of languages and lexemes con fi rm the presence of large numbers of motivated form-meaning units across unrelated, spoken languages (Blasi et al. 2016; Johansson 2017; Johansson et al. 2020; Wichmann et al. 2010). An important contribution of these recent typological studies is that they identify widelyshared tendencies in form-meaning units as proof of the signi fi cance of iconic motivation for human languages. This leaves open the question of crosslinguistic variation in iconic forms; what it looks like and what drives it.

1 Dingemanse (2011) presents an analysis of iconicity types in 219 ideophones in Siwu (Ghana) out of which only 8% are quali fi ed as imagic iconicity.

Sign languages, with their abundance of imagic iconic mappings, enable us to study the rich potential and variation of iconicity in human languages. This study aims at identifying patterning in cross-linguistic variation in imagic iconic signs.

There seem to be language-specific preferences with regard to iconicity in sign languages (Nyst 2007, 2018; Padden et al. 2013). Such iconic preferences may exist in spoken languages as well, but they are perhaps harder to study due to the much more marginal use of imagic iconicity in the lexicon as compared to in sign languages.

## 1.1 Complexity in imagic iconicity in sign languages

Various attempts have been made to create models to broadly capture and categorize iconic signs (i.e. communicative body movements that are part of SLs, mainly used by Deaf signers) and gestures (i.e. communicative body movements mainly used by hearing speakers). For an extensive discussion of these models, see Nyst (2016a). Iconicity in signs is not only highly frequent, but also highly complex, multi-layered and polyvalent. Occhino (2017) discusses the ' many-to-one ' mappings found in iconic signs illustrated by a detailed analysis of the six different iconic mappings that the claw handshape participates in American SL (ASL) and Brazilian SL (Libras) (Figure 1).

Nyst (2016a) argues that a descriptively adequate model of iconicity needs to acknowledge the highly compositional character of iconicity in many signs. Thus, in the sign TOOTHBRUSH (see fi rst picture in Figure 2), the index depicts a toothbrush through embodiment, but the horizontal orientation of the index, as well as its movement and its location, also contribute to the depiction. As a result, iconic signs or gestures are a challenge, as they often do not fi t into one category in their entirety. Nyst (2016a) aims at proposing an exhaustive taxonomy for one of the main domains of iconicity in signs, i.e. size and shape depiction. Rather than proposing a taxonomy for entire signs, it proposes a compositional categorization whereby iconic components of a sign can be coded independently. ## 1.2 Patterning in iconicity

Iconicity in signs is not only complex; often several options exist to iconically depict one and the same concept. A growing body of literature addresses the question of what drives the iconic mapping process in signs and to what extent can we identify patterning in these mappings. Hwang et al. (2017) fi nd that, crosslinguistically, the selection of iconic strategies is biased by semantic fi eld. Thus, the signers of eight SLs prefer the strategy of personi fi cation for the semantic domain of animals. In addition to patterning shared across SLs, studies have also found intra- and inter-linguistic variation in iconic patterning, notably in the distribution of object and handling handshapes (Figure 2, fi rst row, borders in bold). Language-speci fi c patterning has also been attested by Nyst (2016a, 2018) in the frequency of size depiction in space through locations and/or relative distance, i.e. size-in-space depiction (Figure 2, second row, borders in bold). Both types of patterning are discussed in more detail below.

## 1.3 Object and handling depiction

Signs can depict objects in various ways. A common way to depict smaller objects, in particular tools, is by depicting the shape of the object either through mimicking the handling of that object, using a handling handshape or by having the hand mimic the object itself, using an object handshape (also referred to as ' instrument ' handshape). Thus, the concept of a screwdriver can be depicted by means of a handling handshape or an object handshape, as depicted in (a) and (b) respectively in Figure 3.

<!-- image -->

The alternation of object and handling handshape is found to correlate with agentivity in verbs of motion and location in various SLs (Perniss 2007; Zwitserlood 2003). More recent studies have found that the alternation also correlates with the depiction of tools versus actions using those tools (Padden et al. 2015). Brentari et al. (2015b) look at the handshape type in act-on verbs with an instrument (e.g. to comb someone ' s hair, i.e. with a comb) and in those without an instrument (e.g. to hit someone, i.e. without an instrument).

They find a correlation between handshape type and the presence or absence of an instrument in act-on verbs, as well as cross-linguistic variation therein between LIS and Hong Kong SL (HKSL, hong1241) on the one hand, and ASL and British SL (BSL, brit1235) on the other. Ortega et al. (2014) fi nd age-based variation in the distribution of representational strategies in signers of Turkish SL (TГјrk Isaret Dili -TID, turk1288), with children showing a stronger preference for the depiction of action over the depiction of (more static) size and shape features than adults. Looking at emerging SLs, Padden et al. (2013) suggest that a preference for handshape type may be among the fi rst elements of an emerging SL to stabilize, even before a lexicon has stabilized. A similar observation is made by Hou (2018) in her study of iconicity in lexical items in the emerging San Juan Quiahije Chatino SL (SJQCSL, chat1269), where she fi nds that handshape types are more conventional than speci fi c handshapes (Hou 2018). For the notion of iconic patterning re fl ecting linguistic categories, Padden et al. (2013) coin the term patterned iconicity .

In addition to patterned iconicity reflecting linguistic categories, there appear to be seemingly unmotivated, language-specific preferences for the distribution of these handshape types as well. Thus, some SLs display a preference for the use of object handshapes when naming objects, e.g. American SL (ASL; amer1248), 3 Al Sayyid Bedouin SL (ABSL; alsa1242), and Italian SL (Lingua Italiana dei Segni -LIS, ital1275), whereas others prefer the use of handling handshapes, i.e. Hong Kong SL, British SL, and New Zealand SL (NZSL, newz1236) (Brentari et al. 2015b; Padden et al. 2013). In an attempt to scale up these observations, Kimmelman et al. (2018) set up a database of coding iconic mapping types for 87 signs in 19 (mostly European) SLs. Their preliminary results indeed suggest considerable variation in the distribution of object and handling handshapes for the semantic domain of instruments ( n = 10). The results also coincide with the results reported above in previous studies for American SL and British SL. For Italian SL, Kimmelman et al. (2018) found no preference for either handling or object depiction, in contrast to Brentari et al. (2015b), who report a preference for object depiction. The results for Portuguese SL (LGP, port1277) do not align with the ones presented in the current study either. For a discussion of the reliability of dictionary data as opposed to experimental data for this kind of study, see Section 4.3. So far, not many hypotheses have been put forward as to what drives the language-speci fi c preferences for the distribution of object and handling handshapes.

Now that several studies have identified cross-linguistic variation in this respect, we need to address the question of whether there is any pattern in the patterning itself. That is, do we see any bias in this distribution, for example one based on geography, language age, community size, or genetic affiliation? Although these factors are often deemed relevant for cross-linguistic variation in SLs, the available evidence does not point in the direction of age and/or community size, as the comparatively old American SL, with its huge language community, patterns like the emerging village SL, ABSL. Similarly, there is no geographical patterning that transpires from the available evidence. Padden et al. (2013) study to what extent preferences in the gestural environment (i.e. the ensemble of gestural practices of hearing, non-signers) could lead to preferences in the SLs emerging in that environment. Their results for Al Sayyid Bedouin SL and American SL suggest that this is unlikely, as these SLs seem to differ signi fi cantly from their gestural environments. However, the number of SLs studied in this respect obviously needs to be expanded to be able to evaluate this idea reliably.

Whatdoesseemtobeapotential bias in the currently available evidence is the genetic affiliation of a SL, whereby genetically affiliated SLs are those that derive from the same ancestor SL. The genetically affiliated BSL and NZSL pattern alike. However, the results in Kimmelman et al. (2018) show opposite preferences for the af fi liated American SL and French SL, but very similar patterns for the af fi liated SLs of Russia and the Baltic states. More research is needed to understand the genetic af fi liation of SLs and their impact on the distribution of iconic strategies. To see to what extent language age and community size impact on the distribution of handling and object depictions, the database with iconic patterns of Kimmelman et al. (2018) cannot be used, as the sample only contains national SLs, with no clear outliers in terms of community size or language age.

## 1.4 Size-in-space depiction

Another type of cross-linguistic variation in the use of iconic handshapes is found in the use of size-in-space depiction (Nyst 2018). This is a size depicting strategy whereby the distance between two points iconically represents a particular size (Nyst 2016a). Four sub-types of size-in-space depiction are presented in the second rowoftheillustrated overview of strategies in Figure 2. In the fi rst sub-type, the two points are realized hand-internally, by the fi ngers and the thumb (2a), in the second sub-type by the two hands (2b), and the third sub-type by the hand(s) and the ground (2c) and in the fourth by the hand(s) and the body (2d). The size of the space between the two elements depicts the (relative) size of a referent.

Handshapes with size-in-space depiction typically form a subset of handling handshapes. Thus, a tea mug can be depicted with a C handshape, showing its cylindrical shape (Figure 4a), 4 or it can be depicted with a fi st, showing the handling of the handle (Figure 4b).

A systematic comparison of dictionary items for on average 266 concepts per language revealed large differences (Nyst 2018). The three European SLs (French SL, Dutch SL, and Australian SL [aust1271]) in the study used size-in-space depiction in 18 -23% of the signs, whereas the three West African SLs (Adamorobe SL [Ghana], Bouakako SL [CГҙte d ' Ivoire], and Malian SL) did so in only 4 -8%ofthe signs.

In contrast to the results of the studies on the distribution of object/handling handshapes, the results on the distribution of size-in-space depiction do show a geographical bias in the frequency of use of size-in-space depiction, with SLs that originated in Western Europe patterning differently from three SLs, unrelated to each other, that originated in West Africa. The West African SLs include two village SLs (one very old and one very young) and one urban SL, suggesting that both age and community size may not be relevant here. The presence of a regional bias in unrelated SLs is difficult to explain without reference to similarities in the gestural environment. Indeed, Nyst (2016b) fi nds related patterning in the co-speech gestures of hearing non-signing speakers of three spoken languages in West Africa as compared to an equivalent data set for Dutch.

## 1.5 Fine-grained, systematic plotting of the iconic use of the handshape in SLs

As compared to most spoken languages, SLs make much more frequent use of imagic iconicity. The previous section has summarized the evidence for cross-linguistic variation in at least two domains of imagic iconicity. This should urgeustocarefully study representational strategies in and across SLs (and possibly spoken language too), using a fi ne-grained, quantitative approach, inventorying the features in which representational strategies may vary within and across SLs.

Adamorobe SL, a gap is found when it comes to handshapes with the thumb opposing the fi ngers, without touching them, such as the C hand (Nyst 2007). It is yet to be established to what extent this is also the case in the other West African SLs.

The cross-linguistic variation found takes the shape of tendencies in distribution, and as such is gradient rather than categorical. For a typology of the use of the hands, it thus seems statistical analyses are needed. This matches well with current trends in linguistic typology, such as in Multivariate Typology proposed in various publications by Bickel, including Bickel and Nichols (2002), Bickel (2010), Bickel et al. (2014), and Distributional Typology (Bickel 2015). Attempting to fi t linguistic features into prede fi ned categories is replaced with a fi ne-grained, statistics-based approach, which aims at identifying more subtle tendencies and patterns and trying to understand what is where and why. In view of the multilayered nature of human language, typological patterns may be less pronounced than the patterns traditionally studied in typology, but may still be crucial for our understanding of the various factors that shape human languages. Careful statistical analyses are needed to make visible weaker patterns and the relations between them. Such an analysis in turn requires an investment into the development of meaningful and testable hypotheses.

With the ' what is where why ' question of Distributional Typology in mind, one may wonder why the three West African SLs make much less use of size-in-space depiction than the SLs of European origin in the study. As mentioned above, one explanation could be that the size-in-space depiction is found more with handling handshapes, and that West African SLs simply happen to have a preference for object depiction. An alternative explanation involves the marginal use of handinternal size-in-space depiction in the size and shape gestures of the gestural environment of the three West African SLs, which in itself seems to correlate with the use of body-based size and shape gestures and signs in three SLs and their gestural environments. A third possibility would take both explanations together, hypothesizing that the marginality of size-in-space depiction, the use of bodybased size and shape depiction, and a preference of object handshapes are all expressions of a higher-level preference for embodied depiction, potentially already present in the gestural environment.

In this study, we will compare the distribution of object and handling handshapes in the depiction of objects in 11, mostly African, SLs, to see to what extent there is cross-linguistic variation between these SLs, and if so, if any patterns in the variation can be found. Secondly, we will see how the results of this study compare to the results of Nyst (2018) on the distribution of size-in-space depiction across SLs, to see to what extent this size-in-space depiction patterns independently of the handling/object handshape distribution.

## 2 Methodology

## 2.1 Languages

Four other SLs are used in Africa, and are either, at least partly, based on American SL (such as Ghanaian SL, or GSL, ghan1235, Langue des Signes de l ' Afrique Francophone -CГҙte d ' Ivoire, or LSAF-CI, in CГҙte d ' Ivoire [Kamei 2006; Sanogo 2012], and Ethiopian SL [or EthSL, ethi1238]), or in fl uenced by it, as in the case of Kenyan SL (or KSL, keny1241 [Morgan et al. 2015]). 8 Finally, two SLs emerged and are used in Europe: Portuguese SL (or LГӯngua gestual portuguesa), probably largely an isolate (Morgado 2011) and NGT (or Nederlandse Gebarentaal , NGT, dutc1253), which is assumed to initially have been in fl uenced by a historic version of French Sign Language (or Langue des Signes FranГ§aise , LSF, fren1243 [Betten 1984]), but it is unclear to what extent. Thus, the sample holds three village SLs with, by de fi nition, a very small community size, i.e. AdaSL, LaSiBo, and NanaSL, and eight SLs with relatively large Deaf communities. Three of the SLs in the sample are very young SLs: LaSiBo and NanaSL, which are emerging in a village, and LGG, emerging at a deaf school.

## 2.2 Participants and task

For each SL, deaf signers were asked to give the sign for the picture they were presented with. For most SLs, data were collected for 15 or more signers, except for LaSiMa ( n = 11) and KSL ( n = 14). For LaSiBo and NanaSL, only six and eight signers participated respectively, due to the limited number of signers of those SLs. For the sake of consistency, for each of the other languages, the responses of 15 respondents were randomly selected and considered for analysis. Signers were presented with 26 pictures, mostly of hand-held objects, out of which eventually 10 were analyzed, as described in Section 2.3. The objects were selected such that they could be represented both by a handling handshape and an object handshape, e.g. a screwdriver, a knife, tomatoes, and an egg. The data were collected by deaf signers and recorded on video.

## 2.3 Coding

Prior to coding, we removed one picture that was often misunderstood according to the data collectors, i.e. the picture of a bar of soap. A quick look at the data also showed that a number of pictures yielded little to no variation in or across SLs, such as the responses to the picture of the pair of scissors, the key and the book. For the feasibility of the coding process, we selected the responses to a subset of 11 pictures with sufficiently varied results. The responses to the picture of a lipstick were so often ambiguous in terms of iconicity that we decided not to take them into account either. As such, we ended up analyzing the responses to 10 pictures: These were the pictures of a carrot, tomatoes, an egg, a spoon, toothbrushes, a comb, a pencil, a mobile phone, a screwdriver and a saw.

The results were coded by co-author Nyst. To test the reliability of the coding, all the responses for four out of the 11 languages were coded by co-author Morgado as well. Where the right and the left hand depicted different parts of an object or different objects, both were coded and taken into account. As a result, one sign could yield two counts, e.g. one for ' object ' and one for ' handling ' .

## 4 Discussion

The results show that the 11 SLs can be divided into three groups, i.e.:

- 1) a group with a preference for object handshapes: AdaSL, NanaSL, 11 and GSL
- 2) a group with a preference for handling handshapes: LSAF in CГҙte d ' Ivoire, LaSiMa, and LGP
- 3) a middle group without a strong preference : KSL, EthSL, NGT, and LGG, and LaSiBo

Comparison of these results with the frequency of use of another representational strategy, i.e. of size-in-space depiction as reported on for NGT, LaSiMa, LaSiBo, and AdaSL in Nyst (2018) suggests that there is no relation between a preference for object handshapes and infrequent use of size-in-space depiction in SLs other than AdaSL. In fact, like AdaSL, LaSiBo and LaSiMa both make very little use of size-in-space depiction, but, unlike AdaSL, they do not show a preference for object depiction.

We will discuss in more depth the results with respect to the distribution of object and handling handshapes (Section 4.1). The results regarding the relation between the use of object and handling handshapes and the use of size-in-space depiction will be discussed in more detail in Section 4.2. We will raise a methodological question regarding the type of data that is most appropriate for typological studies on iconicity in lexical signs in Section 4.3.

## 4.1 Cross-linguistic patterning in the use of object and handling depiction

The results for object and handling handshapes in the 11 SLs confirm the existence of significant variation between SLs. As discussed in the Introduction, a language-specific preference for a particular distribution could result from a number of motivations, including geographical origin/gestural environment, genetic affiliation (i.e. inheritance), community size and language age. Apart from some evidence for the relevance of genetic affiliation, the results from previous studies do not point to a role for community size and age, or gestural environment. In this section, we will discuss what the results of the present study tell us about the relevance of these factors.

11 The preference for object handshapes in NanaSL was not statistically signi fi cant.

Firstly, the results of this study are not very informative with regard to the role of genetic affiliation . We know that some SLs have a shared ancestor language and can thus be modeled into SL families, just like for many spoken languages. However, we do not know what that means for patterns in iconicity. When a new SL splits off, can it also diverge from its related languages in its use of iconicity? So far, weonlyseemtohaveevidenceofthecontrary:researchbyBrentarietal.(2015b)on British SL and by Padden et al. (2013) on New Zealand SL show that these two related SLs both have a preference for handling handshapes. In our study, we tried to present data on African SLs that originated in Africa as well as outside of it. As a result, the SLs in the data set are quite diverse in terms of genetic af fi liation, with the potential exception of EthSL, GSL, LSAF-CГҙte d ' Ivoire, and KSL. These SLs are based on or are considerably in fl uenced by American SL. These four SLs pattern rather differently from each other, with GSL presenting a strong preference for object depiction, LSAF-CГҙte d ' Ivoire one for handling depiction and EthSL and KSL not showing a signi fi cant preference for either depiction type. This is not in line with the (limited) evidence available thus far for BSL and New Zealand SL. The history of the African SLs with ASL in fl uence in our sample differs considerably from that of New Zealand SL, in that the former all evolved into national SLs out of contact between forms of ASL and forms of local signing. More research is needed onthe history and linguistic structure of these four African SLs (and others) to get a better understanding of the in fl uence of ASL and local SLs on their structure, iconicity, and lexicons.

The results do allow an evaluation of the overlap in preferences in the gestural environment and in the SLs emerging in it. The results from Padden et al. (2013) for Al Sayyid Bedouin SL and American SL suggest that the patterning in these SLs is independent from that in their gestural environments. In contrast, Nyst (2019), discussing the results for size depiction in the lexicons of four West African SL and three SLs of European origin and their gestural environments, shows that there is a direct correlation between the patterns in the iconic use of the hand in SLs and their gestural environments. Moreover, the patterning observed suggests that West Africa functions as one type of gestural area (with all hearing, non-signing participants using body-based size and shape gestures), and the UK and the Netherlands as another one (with no hearing participants using body-based size and shape gestures), and France and Macedonia as a third one (with older hearing participants using body-based size and shape gestures, and younger ones not). In contrast, the present study fi nds no evidence for a coherent areal patterning: LaSiMa shows a preference for handling handshapes, LGG and LaSiBo demonstrate no signi fi cant preference, and AdaSL, NanaSL and GSL show a clear and signi fi cant preference for object handshapes. We do see some regional patterning, however, on a smaller scale; all three Ghanaian SLs pattern more or less alike, with a clear preference for object depiction. Thus, an explanation in terms of gestural environment may hold for a much smaller area, i.e. that of (Southern) Ghana.

A third factor potentially explaining patterns in the distribution of preferences could be the age of the SL , whereby an emerging SL may pattern differently from more established ones. This is not what is re fl ected in the data, as both the young NanaSL and the old AdaSL display a similar distribution of object and handling handshapes.Indeed,amarginalroleforlanguageageinthepatterningoficonicityis in line with the early stabilization of iconic handshape types (as compared to actual handshapes) as observed in Al Sayyid Bedouin SL (Padden et al. 2013) and in San Juan Quiahije Chatino SL (Hou 2018). Similarly, the size of the community -often mentionedasrelevantforSLproperties -doesnotcomeoutasarelevantfactor,with small SLs like NanaSL and AdaSL patterning like an urban SL such as GSL.

## 4.2 Object and handling depiction: independent from size-inspace depiction

The lack of a preference for object depiction in LaSiMa and LaSiBo in the current study was an unexpected result, based on previous studies. In light of the similarities in the iconic use of the hands in AdaSL, LaSiBo, and LaSiMa found in Nyst (2018), and the earlier observation of a preference for object depiction in AdaSL lexemes (Nyst 2007), we expected to see a similar preference for object depiction in LaSiMa and LaSiBo as well. In fact, we expected there to be an areal effect, potentially also re fl ected in LGG. The results, however, show a very different picture. They show that SLs with a low frequency of size-in-space depiction are found in a SL with a strong preference for object depiction (AdaSL), but also in SL with a strong preference for handling depiction (LaSiMa). Frequent (i.e. around 20%) use of size-in-space depiction is also found in a SL with no strong preference for either handling or object depiction (i.e. NGT). It is likely that we will also fi nd other combinations, e.g. in ASL a strong preference for object depiction (cf. Padden et al. 2013; Brentari et al. 2015b) with frequent use of size-in-space depiction, or in British SL and New Zealand SL a strong preference for handling depiction (cf. Brentari et al. 2015b; Padden et al. 2013 resp.) and frequent use of size-in-space depiction.

This unexpected result emphasizes the importance of a careful, fine-grained, and compositional approach to the iconic use of the hands in SLs. As mentioned earlier, handshapes with size-in-space depiction typically form a subset of handling handshapes.

In view of the preference for object depiction in AdaSL reported in Nyst (2007), and the low counts for size-in-space depiction in AdaSL, LaSiMa and LaSiBo (Nyst 2018), we expected the two features to be part of a more general preference for iconic use of the hands, something like a general avoidance of referent projection in space. The -unexpected -preference for handling depiction in LaSiMa and the lack of a signi fi cant preference in LaSiBo suggests that these SLs have nothing against projecting virtual objects in space. More importantly, it shows that a language-speci fi c preference for object or handling depiction is independent of preferences regarding the use of size-in-space depiction. In other words, language-speci fi c patterning in iconicity seems to operate on the level of sub-components, affecting single representational strategies. As such, the results validate the psychological reality of the individual representational strategies.

The small number of studies on patterns in hand iconicity, within and across SLs, have mostly focused on two-way distinctions, such as the use of object or handling handshapes, the depiction of either action or perceptual features (Ortega et al. 2014), or the presence or absence of size-in-space depiction. The current fi ndings, however, underline the need for a new approach to understand cross-linguistic patterning in linguistic iconicity, one that a) acknowledges the compositionality of iconicity in signs, and b) a careful, statistical approach that enables us to catch meaningful differences in frequency. This in turn will shed light on the process underlying lexical iconicity in human languages.

## 4.3 Scaling up cross-linguistic comparisons: experimental or dictionary data

Another aspect that needs deliberation concerns methodologies for data collection. For statistical, cross-linguistic studies of iconicity in SLs to be effective, large datasets of large numbers of languages are needed. Compared to what is available for spoken languages, there is an obvious bottleneck for typological studies into the iconic use of the hands in SLs. Most studies on the distribution of object and handling handshapes in SLs are based on data collected with picture tasks, often comparing different groups (e.g. signers and non-signers, or children and adults). These studies usually feature between 6 and 13 adult signers. 12

12 Paddenet al. (2013) had 12 ASL, seven NZSL, and nine ABSL signers. Padden et al. (2015) had 13 ASLsigners. Hwang et al. (2017) had eight signers for ASL, Central Taurus SL (CTSL, cent2319), and Japanese SL (NS, japa1238). Brentari et al. (2015a) had six ASL and six Italian SL signers. Brentari et al. (2015b) had 10 signers for each of the four SLs involved.

The results presented in this article are based on a set of 11 sign languages, with about 15 signers, and 10 items. For a proper typological study of the iconic use of the hands, a much larger number of languages and items needs to be analyzed, and for a larger number of features. Large scale, typological studies are hardly feasible with an exclusively experimental approach. Indeed, Hwang et al. (2017) in their study of iconic patterning across semantic domains in eight SLs used a mix of different types of data, including experimental data collecting with one signer per SL, dictionary-based data for another SL, and data from a lexical database for yet another SL. This raises the question of which type of data is most representative of actual distribution patterns, and which type of data enable comparisons of large numbers of SLs.

In order to scale up to fine-grained, statistical studies of the iconic use of hands across SLs we need to carefully evaluate the methodologies used so far, and see which type of data are needed to answer which types of typological questions. The advantage of an experimental set up is the consistency and hence comparability of the data. However, for cross-linguistic comparisons, it is debatable -and to some extent an empirical question -how an experimental study compares to a study using data from a lexicographic source. As mentioned in the Introduction, the Iconicity Patterns database based on the spreadthesign.org website presented distribution patterns in line with previous studies for two SLs, but diverging patterns from a previous study and the current study for two other SLs. Ideally, a descriptive reference dictionary represents the lexical items used in the language for a given concept, including variants, if they are in use. Obviously, a lot of current SL dictionaries cannot be considered representative of the existing variation. Also, many dictionaries are not suitable for use in cross-linguistic studies due to the limited number of concepts represented. However, in principle, a quality SL dictionary, developed by linguists and based on extensive lexicographic research, mayprovide reliable and representative data for the cross-linguistic comparison of representational strategies in the lexicons of SLs. A drawback of a dictionary-based approach would be that one loses access to information about frequency in the case of the existence of multiple options. This drawback may be fully compensated, however, by the possibilities that dictionary-based studies open up for scaling up the scope of comparisons. Suppose quality lexicographic resources can indeed be taken to represent the variants found in the lexicon. In that case, the effort invested in recording a limited number of concepts with a limited number of signers can instead be invested in coding a large number of lexical items for a large number of languages.

To shed light on the representativity of lexicographic data as compared to experimental data, we compared the results of our experimental studies with the data presented in quality dictionaries, where available. We only considered dictionaries compiled by linguists. In the case of LaSiMa, the dictionary consulted only contained three of the 10 target items, so we decided not to include this dictionary in the comparison. We ended up comparing our data with data from four SL dictionaries. The results are shown in Table 3.

The advantage of dictionary data is that they present a clearly defined, accessible data set, yielding replicable studies. As such, they offer an opportunity for efficiently comparing large numbers of SLs.

13 The case of LaSiMa, however, reveals another disadvantage of dictionaries, i.e. the limited set of available and/or shared entries. This latter disadvantage may be solved when designing research methods speci fi cally for dictionary-based comparisons. Such comparisons should involve large numbers of items. Due to the impact of variation in culture, agriculture, nature, economic activities, and so on, on the lexicon and its iconicity in SLs, large-scale dictionary-based comparisons will need to de fi ne their selection of signs at the level of the semantic domain, rather than at the level of individual concepts (cf. Nyst 2018).

At the same time, experimental data could be used in a more meaningful way if we try to distinguish between conventionalized, lexicalized signs and nonlexicalized, productive/depictive signs. Although the distinction is not always clear-cut, the former are characterized by a fixed form and a specialized meaning, which the latter lack. Indeed, some items in the current data set turned out to yield highly consistent results within a language, whereas others yielded much more variable results. For example, the sign for PENCIL in LGP was identical with all signers, but the sign for SCREWDRIVER was much more variable in that data set. This suggests that in developing a method for data collection, we should distinguish between common, highly conventionalized items -which can be more ef fi ciently studied by using dictionaries -and non-lexicalized items. Studying variable, nonlexicalized items as separate from lexicalized, stable ones actually has the potential of revealing two different pieces of the puzzle. The lexicalized ones show us whataSLhassettledfor. The non-lexicalized ones show us to what extent there is a shared tendency to use a particular patterning in the iconic use of hands. In other words, the lexicon of a SL offers a standard way (or more than one) in which an item can be represented, but in addition the SL seems to offer standards for creating novel, one-off signs for depicting entities. Zwitserlood (2003) tested the presence of such standards for verbs of location and motion, a.k.a. classi fi er constructions, by presenting signers with images of the location and movement of unusual or non-existing entities. This triggered the use of novel constructions in signers. A similar approach showing novel hand-held tools and other categories would probably trigger novel, ad-hoc signs as well, shedding light on patterning in sign creation and the cross-linguistic variation in it.

In sum, the current, quantitative study has shown that there is crosslinguistic variation in the distribution of object and handling handshapes across sign languages. The language-specific patterns are found not to match with another iconic pattern across SLs, i.e. the use of size-in-space depiction. This shows that the selection of the many representational strategies available to us humans to manually depict a referent may pattern in a language-specific way, independently from each other . For an in-depth understanding of the crosslinguistic patterns in iconic uses of the hands, we should systematically develop and test con fl icting and complementary hypotheses about the forces that shape the particular con fi guration of representational strategies in a sign. These may be manifold, e.g. including not only traditional linguistic functional categories, such as agentivity, instrumentality, or the Noun-Verb distinction, and semantic fi eld (Hwang et al. 2017; Kendon 1980), but also visual saliency and languagespeci fi c prototypical conceptualizations. More research, using more signs and more sign languages is needed to see whether language-speci fi c patterning is also found in other representational strategies, e.g. in the use of tracing movements, or body-based size depiction. We argue that such large scale, typological studies require us to explore more ef fi cient methods for data collection, such as using dictionaries and lexical databases.

## 5 Conclusions

The cross-linguistic data confirm the presence of cross-linguistic variation in the distribution of handling and object depiction in a sample of eight African SLs and two European SLs. The data confirm the preference for object depiction in AdaSL claimed by Nyst (2007). Interestingly, the results are not in line with a scenario in whichlocally evolved West African SLs pattern alike in representational strategies (cf. Nyst 2018). However, they do suggest a more local, Ghanaian pattern instead.

The results furthermore suggest that the age of a SL does not seem to play a role, as the two emerging village SLs do not pattern alike, with NanaSL using object handshapes in 75% of the responses and LaSiBo using handling or object handshapes in roughly 50% of the cases. Importantly, a comparison of the results of this study with an earlier study (Nyst 2018) shows that various iconic features of (lexical) signs may show language-speci fi c patterning, independently from each other. In particular, the use of object and handling handshapes is found not to correlate with the distributional preferences with respect to size-in-space depiction. This fi nding underlines the need for a systematic and fi ne-grained, statistical approach to cross-linguistic variation in the use of meaningful hands in SLs. Such anapproachcalls for the development of more relevant and testable hypotheses on the patterns that may exist in the distribution of iconic features in signs, ultimately shedding light on the processes that shape the linguistic use of iconicity in human languages.

The lexicons of spoken languages and sign languages show us that iconicity is a powerful resource for human languages. Due to the greater affordance of the visual-manual modality for imagic iconicity, SLs enable us to witness and study the patterns yielded by the iconic mapping process directly and on a large scale. As such, studies on iconicity open a window on systematicity and the degree and types of language-specific variation found in linguistic iconicity.
