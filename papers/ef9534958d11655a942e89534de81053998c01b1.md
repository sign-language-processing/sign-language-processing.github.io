## Abstract

Sign language is the primary communication mode for 72 million hearing-impaired individuals worldwide, necessitating effective bidirectional Sign Language Production and Sign Language Translation systems. However, functional bidirectional systems require a unified linguistic environment, hindered by the lack of suitable unified datasets, particularly those providing the necessary pose information for accurate Sign Language Production (SLP) evaluation. Concurrently, current SLP evaluation methods like back-translation ignore pose accuracy, and high-quality coordinated generation remains challenging. To create this crucial environment and overcome these challenges, we introduce CNText2Sign and CNSign, which together constitute the first unified dataset aimed at supporting bidirectional accessibility systems for Chinese sign language; CNText2Sign provides 15,000 natural language-to-sign mappings and standardized skeletal keypoints for 8,643 vocabulary items supporting pose assessment. Building upon this foundation, we propose the AuraLLM model, which leverages a decoupled architecture with CNText2Sign's pose data for novel direct gesture accuracy assessment. The model employs retrieval augmentation and Cascading Vocabulary Resolution to handle semantic mapping and out-of-vocabulary words and achieves all-scenario production with controllable coordination of gestures and facial expressions via pose-conditioned video synthesis. Concurrently, our Sign Language Translation model SignMST-C employs targeted self-supervised pretraining for dynamic feature capture, achieving new SOTA results on PHOENIX2014-T with BLEU-4 scores up to 32.08. AuraLLM establishes a strong performance baseline on CNText2Sign with a BLEU-4 score of 50.41 under direct evaluation.

## TL;DR

The AuraLLM model is proposed, which leverages a decoupled architecture with CNText2Sign's pose data for novel direct gesture accuracy assessment and achieves all-scenario production with controllable coordination of gestures and facial expressions via pose-conditioned video synthesis.

# Beyond Words: AuralLLM and SignMST-C for Precise Sign Language Production and Bidirectional Accessibility

Yulong Li ∗1 , Yuxuan Zhang ∗2 , Feilong Tang 3 ,Mian Zhou 4 , Zhixiang Lu 5 ,Haochen Xue 6 ,Yifang Wang 7 Kang Dangot 8† , Jionglong Su 9† , 1,2,4,5,6,7,8,9 School of Artificial Intelligence and Advanced Computing, Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool University, Suzhou, 215123, China 3 Monash University, Melbourne, Australia {Kang.Dang, Jionglong.Su}@xjtlu.edu.cn

###### Abstract

Although sign language recognition aids non-hearing-impaired understanding, many hearing-impaired individuals still

rely on sign language alone due to limited literacy, underscoring the need for advanced sign language production and

translation (SLP and SLT) systems. In the field of sign language production, the lack of adequate models and datasets

restricts practical applications. Existing models face challenges in production accuracy and pose control, making it

difficult to provide fluent sign language expressions across diverse scenarios. Additionally, data resources are scarce,

particularly high-quality datasets with complete sign vocabulary and pose annotations.

To address these issues, we introduce CNText2Sign and CNSign, comprehensive datasets to benchmark SLP and SLT, respectively,

with CNText2Sign covering gloss and landmark mappings for SLP, and CNSign providing extensive video-to-text data for SLT.

To improve the accuracy and applicability of sign language systems, we propose the AuraLLM and SignMST-C models. AuraLLM,

incorporating LoRA and RAG techniques, achieves a BLEU-4 score of 50.41 on the CNText2Sign dataset, enabling precise control

over gesture semantics and motion. SignMST-C employs self-supervised rapid motion video pretraining, achieving a BLEU-4

score of 31.03/32.08 on the PHOENIX2014-T benchmark, setting a new state-of-the-art. These models establish robust baselines

for the datasets released for their respective tasks.

Approximately 72 million individuals worldwide with hearing impairments rely on sign language

for daily communication [

[49](#bib.bib49) , [35](#bib.bib35) ] . Recent advancements in sign language

recognition technology primarily assist non-hearing-impaired individuals in understanding sign

language content, thus reducing language barriers in communication. However, these recognition

technologies often fail to meet the communication needs of hearing-impaired individuals. Due to

limited educational and resource access, their reading proficiency is often constrained, making

written text less intuitive [

[26](#bib.bib26) , [23](#bib.bib23) ] . In contrast, sign

language production (SLP) technology can directly convert spoken language into sign language videos,

thereby better fulfilling their communication needs [

[37](#bib.bib37) , [53](#bib.bib53) , [56](#bib.bib56) ] .

The goal of SLP is to convert text input into human-like sign language expressions. Current deep learning-based SLP

methods typically involve multiple steps: transforming text to gloss, converting gloss to poses, and rendering these

poses into sign language videos [

[21](#bib.bib21) , [42](#bib.bib42) , [43](#bib.bib43) , [46](#bib.bib46) , [47](#bib.bib47) , [52](#bib.bib52) , [57](#bib.bib57) ] .

However, due to the complexity of this process, these methods require large, high-quality datasets, which exposes

three major limitations in existing datasets and back-translation evaluations for SLP [

[7](#bib.bib7) , [29](#bib.bib29) , [59](#bib.bib59) , [19](#bib.bib19) , [18](#bib.bib18) ] . First, the absence of a foundational lexicon and corresponding videos results in a

lack of consistent intermediate representations, making it challenging to maintain semantic coherence. Second,

limited scene coverage in current datasets restricts model performance when generating expressions for out-of-vocabulary

terms. Third, back-translation evaluation, which relies solely on sentence-level comparisons, cannot accurately assess

the precision of individual gestures or poses, often masking errors in sign pose accuracy.

To address the limitations of current SLP datasets, we introduce CNText2Sign, a new large-scale

Chinese natural language-to-sign gloss mapping dataset. CNText2Sign comprises 15,000 annotated mappings from natural

language to sign glosses, curated by professional sign language experts, and includes 8,643 officially defined core

Chinese sign vocabulary items, each accompanied by corresponding video data. By utilizing OpenPose [

[6](#bib.bib6) ] for skeleton

keypoint extraction, we generate complete temporal pose sequences, establishing a consistent intermediate representation

that enhances semantic coherence in SLP models. This lexicon and annotation structure allows for fine-grained control

over semantic and emotional expression, addressing the consistency issues stemming from the limited vocabulary in

previous datasets. Additionally, we introduce CNSign, a dataset that bridges the data scarcity gap in Chinese sign language

translation (SLT). CNSign consists of 10,943 video-to-text mappings, with video durations ranging from 5 to 32 seconds,

sourced primarily from real-world news scenarios and incorporating complex sentence structures suitable for a wide range

of practical applications. Together, these datasets provide comprehensive gloss and pose data for SLP tasks, addressing

the critical limitations of previous resources and ensuring model adaptability across diverse real-world scenarios,

including daily conversations, news broadcasts, and public services, thereby enabling effective sign language production

and translation across multiple contexts.

Bidirectional sign language translation systems, encompassing sign-to-text (SLT) and text-to-sign (SLP) tasks, face

several critical challenges. First, existing SLT methods struggle with aligning dynamic visual gestures and linguistic

semantics, leading to difficulties in capturing fine-grained motion details and producing grammatically correct, semantically

coherent text [

[3](#bib.bib3) ] . Second, SLP systems often generate videos that lack natural motion

fluency and fail to convey complex semantics and emotional nuances effectively [

[15](#bib.bib15) ] .

Third, SLT and SLP are typically studied as independent tasks, lacking an integrated design that addresses the interconnections

required for real-world applications [

[15](#bib.bib15) ] .

To address these challenges, we propose AuraLLM and SignMST-C as part of a unified framework for bidirectional sign language

translation. SignMST-C leverages self-supervised pretraining on rapid motion videos and multimodal feature fusion to enhance

SLT performance, ensuring precise semantic alignment and dynamic motion comprehension. AuraLLM incorporates LoRA fine-tuning

and RAG-based retrieval to achieve accurate text-to-sign mapping, generating natural and fluent sign language videos through

the SL-ControlNet module. As illustrated in Figure 1, our approach systematically integrates SLT and SLP into a complete

bidirectional translation pipeline, bridging the gap between sign language and natural language while providing a unified

framework to support diverse application scenarios effectively.

The key contributions of this paper are three-fold:

1) We propose the first high-precision sign language production model, AuraLLM, which supports production across all daily scenarios.

Through strong supervision, AuraLLM achieves fine control over hand poses, ensuring the accuracy and naturalness of the sign language

video gestures. In the text-to-gloss task, serving as a baseline, AuraLLM achieves a BLEU-4 score of 50.41%.

2) We introduce a self-supervised pretraining method focused on rapid motion video, specifically designed for SLT tasks.

This method incorporates a self-supervised pretraining strategy with weighted perturbation processing based on landmark data, enhancing

feature learning in dynamic areas and significantly improving model performance. We achieved a 2.37/3.13 increase on the BLEU-4 metric, establishing a new SOTA.

3) We also collect and release the largest dataset to date specifically for Chinese sign language production, CNText2Sign, along with a Chinese sign language

translation dataset, CNSign. The CNText2Sign dataset includes an extensive vocabulary and corresponding landmark data, providing accurate

vocabulary-to-pose mappings that enable the model to generate sign language videos with high semantic consistency and pose precision.

## 2 Related Work

### Sign Language Datasets

In recent years, research on SLP [ [13](#bib.bib13) , [24](#bib.bib24) , [30](#bib.bib30) , [31](#bib.bib31) , [50](#bib.bib50) ] has

gradually shifted from traditional animation synthesis methods to data-driven approaches based on deep learning.

However, its development is limited by the lack of high-quality sign language datasets, particularly in non-English

sign languages. Existing datasets are mostly focused on a few languages, such as American Sign Language (ASL)

and German Sign Language (GSL), and often restricted to specific topics, such as weather and daily phrases,

which limits their applicability to a broader range of contexts [

[22](#bib.bib22) , [41](#bib.bib41) , [44](#bib.bib44) , [45](#bib.bib45) , [47](#bib.bib47) ] .

Most datasets provide videos of isolated vocabulary or short phrases, lacking complex syntax and diverse

contexts, making it challenging for generative models to produce natural and coherent sign language expressions

in more varied situations [

[14](#bib.bib14) ] . Moreover, the commonly used back-translation method to evaluate sign

language production has its limitations, as it struggles to capture the nuanced semantics and grammatical

features of sign language [

[2](#bib.bib2) , [12](#bib.bib12) , [5](#bib.bib5) , [25](#bib.bib25) ] . This issue is particularly pronounced in the domain

of Chinese Sign Language (CSL), where existing datasets mostly consist of isolated words or basic phrases,

lacking support for complex sentences and diverse scenes [

[15](#bib.bib15) ] . Our work introduces the CNText2Sign dataset, which

fills these gaps by offering multi-modal annotations, a large vocabulary, and broad contextual coverage.

It supports more comprehensive sign language production across various scenarios.

### Large Language Model in SLP

Large Language Model have demonstrated significant potential in SLP tasks

due to their powerful natural language processing capabilities [

[4](#bib.bib4) , [11](#bib.bib11) , [51](#bib.bib51) , [54](#bib.bib54) , [55](#bib.bib55) ] . However,

sign language, as a visual language, exhibits structural characteristics that differ significantly from the

textual information processed by language models, presenting challenges in applying LLMs to the SLP domain.

Current research typically applies LLMs by converting visual features of sign language, such as keypoints or

skeletal poses, into sequential data for text-based pose generation, resembling a language modeling approach [

[27](#bib.bib27) , [38](#bib.bib38) , [40](#bib.bib40) , [40](#bib.bib40) ] .

Since the vocabulary of sign language is much smaller than that of natural

languages, LLMs often simplify details and lose information during the generation process, particularly in terms

of emotions, intonation, and complex syntax, which lack direct counterparts in sign language. Some studies

have attempted to combine LLMs with multimodal models by incorporating pose keypoints or gloss vocabulary as

intermediate layers. However, the generation results still heavily depend on the LLM, and the design of prompts

greatly impacts the output quality [

[4](#bib.bib4) , [11](#bib.bib11) , [39](#bib.bib39) ] . Furthermore, sentences generated by LLMs often

retain the syntax of natural language, which is not suitable for the concise expression style of sign language.

To address these challenges, we introduce a multi-level Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) technique that

dynamically retrieves and generates multimodal information suitable for sign language, enabling the model to

accurately express details and emotions from natural language.

### SLT and Advances in Gloss-Free Approaches

In recent years, to improve the effectiveness of SLT, many studies adopted sign

gloss as an intermediate representation layer. SLRT [

[5](#bib.bib5) ] first introduced a Transformer-based encoder-decoder

structure combined with Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) loss to align sign language representations with

gloss sequences, significantly enhancing translation performance. However, the process of obtaining gloss annotations

is complex and costly. To address this, STMC-T [

[61](#bib.bib61) ] proposed multi-stream learning, using both single-stream

and cross-stream CTC losses to model sequence information more accurately, although this method has limited performance

when dealing with complex syntax. SignBack [

[60](#bib.bib60) ] introduced back-translation techniques to improve the linguistic

expressiveness of SLT, but back-translation struggles to ensure word-level precision, particularly in nuanced semantic

expressions. Chen et al. [

[9](#bib.bib9) , [10](#bib.bib10) ] explored the application of Large Language Models (LLMs) in SLT, leveraging

the powerful generative capabilities of LLMs to enhance translation accuracy. However, LLMs still face challenges in

aligning visual and linguistic modalities. Recently, gloss-free SLT has emerged as a new approach, with NSLT [

[12](#bib.bib12) ] utilizing a CNN+RNN architecture for end-to-end SLT. Despite its advancements, it still faces limitations in semantic

and motion alignment due to the absence of intermediate representations. TSPNet [

[28](#bib.bib28) ] introduced a cross-scale

attention mechanism to strengthen the capture of visual features, while CSGCR [

[58](#bib.bib58) ] improved SLT accuracy and

fluency through a multi-module design. Nevertheless, challenges in cross-modal alignment remain unresolved. To address

these issues, we propose an end-to-end SLT model combining self-supervised pretraining.

## 3 Dataset Benchmark

Chinese Natural Language to Sign Language Gloss Mapping Dataset (CNText2Sign) The CNText2Sign dataset is an innovative resource for SLP tasks, featuring 15,000 mappings from

Chinese natural language to sign language gloss, each annotated by professionals to ensure accuracy and consistency.

It also includes 8,643 basic Chinese sign language vocabulary items with corresponding videos, processed through

MediaPipe [

[6](#bib.bib6) ] to standardize skeleton keypoints and create complete sign language pose sequences. This multimodal

data provides an end-to-end, standardized training pipeline that enables precise semantic control in video

generation. CNText2Sign was created by collecting extensive video materials from public platforms and supplementing

missing vocabulary based on the official Chinese sign language guide, with trained experts contributing annotations.

MediaPipe [

[6](#bib.bib6) ] processing extracts 3D skeletal keypoints, further converted to JSON and H5 formats, ensuring data

efficiency and usability, thus providing essential gesture information for SLP models.

Dataset Gap Filling

CNText2Sign is the largest Chinese natural language to sign language gloss mapping dataset currently available,

covering all officially defined Chinese sign language vocabulary, as given in Table 1. It supports

rich semantic expressions in SLP models. The dataset is suitable for full-scenario sign language production tasks,

covering various real-world application scenarios, including daily conversations, news reports, public services,

etc. Providing standardized data simplifies the sign language production process and helps improve the

model's generation quality and adaptability in complex situations.

Chinese Sign Language to Natural Language Dataset (CNSign)

To address the scarcity of Chinese SLT data, we have released a dataset with 10,943

entries translating Chinese sign language videos into natural language, bridging the gap in video-to-text translation.

The dataset's video samples, primarily sourced from the Chinese news program "Focus On", feature realistic contexts

and complex syntax, with video durations ranging from 5 to 32 seconds to support diverse sentence structures and

enhance model adaptability. Due to the typical 5-10 second translation lag, alignment between video and text was

manually performed by seven trained experts to ensure high accuracy and coherence, providing reliable annotated

data for model training, validation, and evaluation.

Dataset Gap Filling

This dataset plays a crucial role in Chinese sign language to natural language translation tasks, filling the

gap for SLT data in diverse real-world scenarios. With rich contextual information and

complex syntactical structures, this dataset is suitable for training SLT models in various scenarios. It is

particularly valuable in improving the accuracy and semantic understanding of models in complex expressions,

such as news reporting.

## 4 Methodology

### SLP Framework

AuraLLM consists of the ZRSL module (Natural Language to Sign Language Conversion Module) and the

SL-ControlNet module, designed to generate highly accurate sign language videos, as seen in Figure 2. The ZRSL module includes a

LoRA fine-tuning combined with a RAG retrieval enhancement module (LoRA+RAG), which relies on a proprietary

sign language vocabulary and landmark data to ensure the accuracy of sign symbol generation. LoRA fine-tuning

enhances the model's ability to generate synonymous expressions aligned with sign language vocabulary, while RAG

retrieval selects the most semantically consistent symbols from the sign vocabulary to ensure translation

precision and semantic consistency. The landmark data within the sign vocabulary details the key poses

associated with each sign symbol, serving as a foundational element for precise sign language video generation.

The ZRSL module produces a symbol sequence that aligns with sign language expression and maps these symbols

to 3D skeletal poses. The SL-ControlNet module then receives this skeletal pose sequence and transforms it

into a natural, smooth video output, ensuring that each sign language gesture transitions seamlessly and

cohesively within the video, thereby achieving high-quality sign language video output from natural language input.

Accurate Sign Language Symbols and Smooth Video Generation

To enable efficient sign symbol generation under multimodal input conditions, we apply LoRA

fine-tuning based on a large language model. LoRA fine-tuning introduces low-rank matrices into the model's weight

matrix, allowing the model to quickly adapt. This approach offers two major advantages: it retains the core

capabilities of the pre-trained model and enhances the model's ability to generate synonyms within the sign

language vocabulary, particularly for words without direct matches.

In the multimodal input process, when certain words in text or audio lack a direct corresponding symbol in the

sign language vocabulary, the system leverages the LoRA fine-tuned model to generate a set of potential synonym

candidates. The system then applies RAG technology, using embeddings to match

these candidates with vocabulary entries in the sign language lexicon, ensuring that the closest sign language

expression is selected. For instance, the embedding representation of an unmatched word q q is matched against

the sign vocabulary embeddings to identify the most semantically similar option. For example, the unmatched word q q is represented by its embedding e  ( q ) e(q) , and a candidate word w i w\_{i} in the sign language vocabulary

is represented by its embedding e  ( w i ) e(w\_{i}) . The semantic similarity between them is calculated using cosine similarity:

and natural sign language actions in the video. The final output video frames exhibit realistic dynamic features in both time

#### SLT Framework

We propose a SignMST-C ( Sign M odel S elf- T ranslation with C orrection)

framework based on multimodal fusion, as seen in Figure 3, which combines

self-supervised pretraining for rapid motion video semantic reconstruction, multimodal feature fusion of

video and landmark data, end-to-end video-to-text translation, and a text correction network. The framework

aims to improve the accuracy and timeliness of translating sign language videos into natural language text.

Below are detailed descriptions and formula definitions for each model.

Self-Supervised Pretraining for Rapid Motion Video Semantic Reconstruction To enhance the model's attention to dynamic regions in sign language videos, we design a self-supervised

pretraining method. By introducing various perturbations to generate noisy input frames and using a weighted

perturbation strategy based on landmark recognition, the model is guided to focus on the dynamic features of

fast-moving regions during the reconstruction process. In this process, we use a ResNet18-3D convolutional

network to extract features and a low-parameter decoder to restore video content, ensuring that the model

focuses on the most critical features.

In the self-supervised input generation phase, various perturbations

are applied to the video frames v t v\_{t} to enhance the model's focus on areas of rapid motion. These perturbations

include Pixel Shuffling, which randomly reorders pixel positions within a frame to disrupt the static spatial

structure of the image; Random Pixel Replacement, which replaces a specified proportion of pixels with random values

to simulate blurring; Block Occlusion, which generates random blocks in the image with pixel values set to zero to

simulate information loss; Local Gaussian Noise, which adds noise sampled from a Gaussian distribution to random

regions to mimic capture interference; and finally, Temporal Sequence Shuffling, which randomly reorders video frames

to disrupt the original temporal sequence.

regions in video frames, applying higher perturbation weights to these areas. The detailed process is as follows:

Multimodal Module for SLT

In the multimodal module of the SLT framework, we concatenate and fuse video and landmark features to enhance the model's

ability to capture spatiotemporal geometric information in sign language videos. To ensure temporal consistency across

different modal features and align the 3D convolutional features with the semantic requirements of the target text, we

designed three distillation losses and a cross-entropy loss to guide model learning.

Video frames are processed through a ResNet18 3D network to extract Conv\_Feature, capturing the spatiotemporal dynamics

of the sign language video. The landmark information in the sign language video is processed through a 1D convolutional

layer, generating 1D Conv Feature, which captures the geometric positional changes of sign language actions, providing

supplementary support for spatiotemporal dynamics.

The video and landmark features are then concatenated and fed into a Transformer, where a multi-layer self-attention

mechanism enables feature interaction and fusion. This process generates the multimodal fused feature representation,

Tran\_Feature. The self-attention mechanism effectively combines the spatial information from the video and the structural

information from the landmark, equipping the model with a robust capacity for dynamic understanding.

In the process of multimodal fusion, the video and landmark features are derived from different networks. To maintain

temporal consistency between these features, we introduce three distillation losses to ensure that the temporal structures

of the features are aligned before and after fusion, thus avoiding information distortion or conflict. These losses

measure the similarity between feature distributions using Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence:

Text Correction Network

The Text Correction Network is designed to enhance the model's capability to identify and correct errors in

preliminary translations, including issues with word order, missing words, redundancies, and other inaccuracies.

To achieve this, we generate training data by introducing artificial errors into correct text, simulating typical

error patterns often made by student networks, as part of the pretraining process.

During pretraining, we employ an error-injection strategy that introduces noise into correct sentences through

random shuffling, deletion, substitution, or insertion of words. This approach allows the model to learn how to

reconstruct accurate sentence structures from noisy inputs, improving its robustness across various error scenarios.

By combining noisy data with authentic text, the model not only maintains semantic comprehension but also progressively

strengthens its sensitivity to grammatical structures, leading to more efficient and precise error correction.

## 6 Conclusion

We introduced high-precision models for sign language production (SLP) and translation (SLT), AuraLLM and SignMST-C,

along with two new Chinese sign language datasets, CNText2Sign and CNSign, addressing the critical shortage of Chinese

sign language resources. AuraLLM leverages LoRA fine-tuning and RAG retrieval to enable efficient text-to-sign conversion,

while SignMST-C establishes a new SLT benchmark on the Phoenix2014-T dataset through self-supervised pretraining and

multimodal feature fusion. To support further advancements in sign language technology and foster inclusive communication,

we will publicly release all data and code.
