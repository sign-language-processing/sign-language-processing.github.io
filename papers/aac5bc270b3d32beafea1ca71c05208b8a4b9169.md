## Abstract

Generating natural and linguistically accurate sign language avatars remains a formidable challenge. Current Sign Language Production (SLP) frameworks face a stark trade-off: direct text-to-pose models suffer from regression-to-the-mean effects, while dictionary-retrieval methods produce robotic, disjointed transitions. To resolve this, we propose a novel training paradigm that leverages sparse keyframes to capture the true underlying kinematic distribution of human signing. By predicting dense motion from these discrete anchors, our approach mitigates regression-to-the-mean while ensuring fluid articulation. To realize this paradigm at scale, we first introduce FAST, an ultra-efficient sign segmentation model that automatically mines precise temporal boundaries. We then present SignSparK, a large-scale Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) framework that utilizes these extracted anchors to synthesize 3D signing sequences in SMPL-X and MANO spaces. This keyframe-driven formulation also uniquely unlocks Keyframe-to-Pose (KF2P) generation, making precise spatiotemporal editing of signing sequences possible. Furthermore, our adopted reconstruction-based CFM objective also enables high-fidelity synthesis in fewer than ten sampling steps; this allows SignSparK to scale across four distinct sign languages, establishing the largest multilingual SLP framework to date. Finally, by integrating 3D Gaussian Splatting for photorealistic rendering, we demonstrate through extensive evaluation that SignSparK establishes a new state-of-the-art across diverse SLP tasks and multilingual benchmarks.

###### Abstract

Generating natural and linguistically accurate sign language avatars remains a formidable challenge. Current Sign Language Production (SLP) frameworks face a stark trade-off: direct text-to-pose models suffer from regression-to-the-mean effects, while dictionary-retrieval methods produce robotic, disjointed transitions. To resolve this, we propose a novel training paradigm that leverages sparse keyframes to capture the true underlying kinematic distribution of human signing. By predicting dense motion from these discrete anchors, our approach mitigates regression-to-the-mean while ensuring fluid articulation. To realize this paradigm at scale, we first introduce FAST, an ultra-efficient sign segmentation model that automatically mines precise temporal boundaries. We then present SignSparK, a large-scale Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) framework that utilizes these extracted anchors to synthesize 3D signing sequences in SMPL-X and MANO spaces. This keyframe-driven formulation also uniquely unlocks Keyframe-to-Pose (KF2P) generation, making precise spatiotemporal editing of signing sequences possible. Furthermore, our adopted reconstruction-based CFM objective also enables high-fidelity synthesis in fewer than ten sampling steps; this allows SignSparK to scale across four distinct sign languages, establishing the largest multilingual SLP framework to date. Finally, by integrating 3D Gaussian Splatting for photorealistic rendering, we demonstrate through extensive evaluation that SignSparK establishes a new state-of-the-art across diverse SLP tasks and multilingual benchmarks.

## 1 Introduction

Sign languages are linguistically rich, natural languages primarily used in Deaf communities, and are governed by precise hand shapes and fluid body dynamics [ [74](#bib.bib74) , [45](#bib.bib45) , [56](#bib.bib56) ] . While Sign Language Translation (SLT) from video-to-text has seen decades of progress [ [10](#bib.bib10) , [96](#bib.bib96) , [26](#bib.bib26) , [44](#bib.bib44) , [48](#bib.bib48) ] , the inverse direction of text-to-video or Sign Language Production (SLP) remains underexplored [ [68](#bib.bib68) , [76](#bib.bib76) , [92](#bib.bib92) , [3](#bib.bib3) , [99](#bib.bib99) ] . Despite recent advancements in video foundation models, synthesizing linguistically accurate signing requires a balance of fine-grained articulation and structured temporal transitions that general human-motion models fail to capture.

Consequently, existing SLP frameworks face a stark dilemma between motion naturalness and linguistic accuracy. Direct Text-to-Pose (T2P) models [ [78](#bib.bib78) , [92](#bib.bib92) , [76](#bib.bib76) , [68](#bib.bib68) ] struggle to map across drastically different modalities, typically suffering from regression-to-the-mean effects that yield under-articulated movements. Conversely, isolated sign dictionary-retrieval methods based on *glosses* 1 1 1 Glosses are the written representations used to denote individual signs. [ [75](#bib.bib75) , [71](#bib.bib71) , [99](#bib.bib99) ] preserve sign articulations but often yield robotic and disjointed transitions, akin to concatenating discrete words without prosody. Furthermore, current SLP paradigms remain bottlenecked by inaccuracies in monocular 3D estimation (e.g., HaMeR [ [55](#bib.bib55) ] ), as the ambiguity of projecting 2D views into 3D space fundamentally limits the quality of the extracted pseudo-ground truth used in training.

To address these challenges, we propose SignSparK ( Sign Language Production with Spar se K eyframes), a large-scale SLP framework driven by a novel *training paradigm* : learning to synthesize continuous signing sequences given spoken text and sparse keyframes as training inputs ( [Fig. ˜ 1](#S1.F1) ). Since the model must explicitly hit these sparse keyframes, they act as anchors that inherently prevent regression-to-the-mean. When applied at scale, SignSparK cannot rely on naive interpolation to resolve the complex non-linearity of signing; thus, it instead needs to learn the underlying distribution of fluid human signing. Crucially, while SignSparK is fully capable of standard end-to-end T2P generation at inference, this unique training strategy also introduces a highly controllable task we refer to as Keyframe-to-Pose (KF2P) synthesis; mirroring the use of keyframes in the animation industry [ [95](#bib.bib95) , [50](#bib.bib50) ] . By conditioning on user-defined keyframes, one can temporally shift anchors to dictate signing speed or seamlessly replace noisy monocular 3D estimates with corrected poses to guarantee articulation accuracy. Furthermore, by incorporating a reconstruction-based Flow Matching objective, SignSparK achieves high-quality synthesis in fewer than ten sampling steps, enabling us to scale to the largest multilingual SLP framework to date, encompassing German, Chinese, American, and British sign languages.

However, keyframe annotations do not exist in current SLP datasets, rendering this training paradigm infeasible. To overcome this, we additionally introduce FAST, an extremely efficient sign segmentation model designed to automatically mine linguistically meaningful temporal anchors at scale. By precisely identifying sign boundaries, FAST provides the foundational keyframe extractions required to train SignSparK. Beyond enabling SignSparK, FAST is also explicitly engineered to process large-scale corpora like BOBSL [ [1](#bib.bib1) ] with minimal computational overhead. It therefore offers immense standalone value for the broader community, as it can facilitate the rapid pseudo-annotation of emerging large-scale glossless datasets like YTSL-25 [ [80](#bib.bib80) ] and CSL-News [ [44](#bib.bib44) ] , and support downstream tasks such as sign spotting [ [83](#bib.bib83) ] and tokenization [ [48](#bib.bib48) ] .

By leveraging the keyframes extracted by FAST in training, SignSparK synthesizes articulate and naturalistic signing sequences directly within the 3D parametric spaces of SMPL-X [ [54](#bib.bib54) ] and MANO [ [64](#bib.bib64) ] . Unlike prior 2D keypoint methods, this 3D formulation offers superior spatial depth and physical plausibility. To round off the SLP pipeline, we further incorporate 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [ [39](#bib.bib39) ] to bypass the visual limitations of bare meshes and seamlessly render the generated kinematics as photorealistic signing avatars. In this work we focus on manual articulators (upper-body + hands) and generating linguistically accurate non-manual markers (mouthings/facial expression) remains future work. In short, the main contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:

- • State-of-the-art segmentation. We introduce FAST, an ultra-efficient sign segmentation model that achieves highly accurate boundary detection at scale and unlocks the lingustic keyframes required for our training paradigm.
- • Novel training paradigm. We propose SignSparK, a generative SLP framework driven by sparse keyframe training. This approach overcomes under-articulation and ensures fluid motion while uniquely unlocking Keyframe-to-Pose (KF2P) synthesis for precise spatial and temporal control.
- • Unprecedented efficiency and scale. We formulate a reconstruction-based Flow Matching objective for SLP and achieve a 100 × 100\times efficiency gain over prior models. We simultaneously leverage this efficiency to establish the largest multilingual SLP framework to date across four major sign languages.
- • State-of-the-art SLP performance. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that SignSparK achieves state-of-the-art performance across a wide range of SLP regimes and sign langauge datasets.
- • Open source release. We will fully release our codebase and pseudo-annotations to catalyze future sign language research upon publication.

## 2 Related Work

*Sign Language Segmentation.* In continuous signing, the fluid transitional movements between consecutive signs, known as *coarticulations* , create smooth motion continuity. Sign language segmentation seeks to temporally localize individual signs within these sequences, while disregarding coarticulatory transitions. Early approaches relied on statistical techniques [ [6](#bib.bib6) , [17](#bib.bib17) , [65](#bib.bib65) ] and machine learning approaches [ [25](#bib.bib25) ] , but struggled with the multimodal, continuous nature of signing. Deep learning approaches later adopted sequential architectures such as the BiLSTM [ [8](#bib.bib8) ] and spatiotemporal models like the I3D [ [13](#bib.bib13) ] to achieve notable improvements [ [62](#bib.bib62) , [63](#bib.bib63) , [7](#bib.bib7) ] . More recent studies, inspired by linguistics, then reframed sign segmentation as a Begin-In-Out (BIO) tagging task [ [51](#bib.bib51) ] . With Hands-On [ [49](#bib.bib49) ] , advancing this task through strong 3D body [ [34](#bib.bib34) ] and hand [ [55](#bib.bib55) ] priors, achieving state-of-the-art on the DGS Corpus [ [42](#bib.bib42) ] . Our method, FAST, further improves this via a unimodal two-stream design and interpolated training, providing superior accuracy and significant efficiency gains.

*Sign Language Production.* Generating naturalistic signing videos or avatars from language or motion-based inputs defines the field of SLP. Early research was filled with graphics-based avatars driven by handcrafted linguistic rules and annotations [ [4](#bib.bib4) , [19](#bib.bib19) , [22](#bib.bib22) , [23](#bib.bib23) , [100](#bib.bib100) ] . Although linguistically structured, these pipelines produced unnatural motions, limiting acceptance within the Deaf community [ [41](#bib.bib41) ] .

The advent of deep learning shifted SLP towards data-driven approaches. Owing to the non-monotonic mapping between sign and spoken languages, many systems introduced glosses as intermediate supervision, forming text-to-gloss-to-pose (T2G2P) frameworks [ [78](#bib.bib78) , [92](#bib.bib92) , [76](#bib.bib76) , [68](#bib.bib68) , [69](#bib.bib69) , [70](#bib.bib70) , [67](#bib.bib67) ] . In contrast, direct regression methods typically predict 2D/3D poses straight from text or linguistic inputs such as HamNoSys [ [2](#bib.bib2) ] or glosses [ [14](#bib.bib14) , [33](#bib.bib33) , [79](#bib.bib79) ] ; however, they frequently produce under-articulated motion due to regression-to-the-mean effects. Alternatives like interpolating isolated signs [ [84](#bib.bib84) , [71](#bib.bib71) ] improve said articulations, but rely on complex pipelines and smoothing filters that fail to capture natural human signing.

To enhance realism, methods employing GANs [ [75](#bib.bib75) , [76](#bib.bib76) , [71](#bib.bib71) ] , diffusion models [ [24](#bib.bib24) , [85](#bib.bib85) ] , and recently 3DGS [ [35](#bib.bib35) ] have also been explored. However, a growing trend now centers on 3D avatar generation, where models predict parametric human body models such as SMPL-X [ [54](#bib.bib54) ] to enable full-body mesh reconstructions [ [3](#bib.bib3) , [91](#bib.bib91) , [20](#bib.bib20) , [99](#bib.bib99) , [5](#bib.bib5) ] . These frameworks span diffusion [ [3](#bib.bib3) , [5](#bib.bib5) ] , VAE [ [20](#bib.bib20) ] , and VQ-VAE [ [91](#bib.bib91) , [99](#bib.bib99) ] paradigms, but they remain largely monolingual and computationally inefficient. To overcome this, Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) has recently emerged as a highly efficient generative alternative, with SignFlow [ [40](#bib.bib40) ] pioneering its use in SLP. However, its resulting synthesis still exhibits clear articulatory inaccuracies and its evaluation remains restricted to a single dataset.

Ultimately, progress in 3D production, multilingual scaling, and inference efficiency has remained highly fragmented. SignSparK instead presents a unified framework to bridge these domains. By integrating a keyframe-based paradigm for fluid articulation with a highly efficient reconstruction-based CFM formulation, as well as realistic rendering via 3DGS, we scale to a massive multilingual setting to provide a practical, high-fidelity system for the Deaf community.

*Human Body Motion Generation.* Human motion generation is a long-standing problem in computer vision, conditioned on diverse modalities such as action labels [ [9](#bib.bib9) , [90](#bib.bib90) , [87](#bib.bib87) , [57](#bib.bib57) ] , audio [ [73](#bib.bib73) , [43](#bib.bib43) ] , and increasingly, text [ [28](#bib.bib28) , [58](#bib.bib58) , [29](#bib.bib29) , [81](#bib.bib81) , [27](#bib.bib27) , [82](#bib.bib82) , [15](#bib.bib15) , [94](#bib.bib94) ] , enabled by large-scale datasets like KIT-ML [ [59](#bib.bib59) ] , BABEL [ [61](#bib.bib61) ] , and HumanML3D [ [28](#bib.bib28) ] . Recent approaches typically represent motion as latent tokens within autoencoder frameworks for autoregressive Transformer-based generation [ [58](#bib.bib58) , [27](#bib.bib27) , [81](#bib.bib81) , [93](#bib.bib93) ] , or leverage diffusion-based models [ [31](#bib.bib31) ] to produce temporally coherent sequences [ [82](#bib.bib82) , [72](#bib.bib72) , [94](#bib.bib94) , [38](#bib.bib38) , [15](#bib.bib15) ] .

However, despite significant progress, human motion generation approaches emphasize full-body motion, and thus neglect the fine-grained hand and finger dynamics essential for sign language production.

*Keyframe In-betweening Generation.* In motion/video synthesis, *keyframe in-betweening* aims to generate intermediate frames given sparse keyframe constraints. Diffusion-based approaches have recently advanced this across domains, including video [ [86](#bib.bib86) , [30](#bib.bib30) , [36](#bib.bib36) , [88](#bib.bib88) ] , human motion [ [38](#bib.bib38) , [82](#bib.bib82) , [16](#bib.bib16) ] , and hand interactions [ [46](#bib.bib46) ] .

However, existing human motion frameworks remain limited: MDM [

[82](#bib.bib82) ] suffers from foot-sliding and unrealistic transitions under keyframe conditioning; GMD [ [38](#bib.bib38) ] handles sparse interpolation but focuses on pelvis trajectories; CondMDI [ [16](#bib.bib16) ] is closest to our setting, but targets general locomotion without explicit hand modeling and relies on a computationally expensive diffusion process. In contrast, our approach enables efficient few-step sampling while explicitly capturing the fine-grained articulations essential for natural signing motion.

## 3 Methodology

This work proposes a sparse keyframe-based SLP training paradigm consisting of two primary components. Firstly, a sign language segmentation model (Sec. [3.1](#S3.SS1) ) that localizes sign boundaries and extracts linguistically meaningful keyframes; and secondly, a CFM model (Sec. [3.2](#S3.SS2) ) that synthesizes smooth and realistic sign language motion conditioned on these sparse keyframes and spoken text input.

### 3.1 Sign Language Segmentation

Sign language segmentation can be formulated as a *BIO-tagging* problem, where each frame is classified as either B (beginning of a sign), I (inside a sign), or O (outside a sign), corresponding to the class indices { 2 , 1 , 0 } \{2,1,0\} . In this setting, given an input video 𝐗 ∈ ℝ T × H × W × C \mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times H\times W\times C} , with T T frames, H × W H\times W spatial resolution, and C C channel dimensions, the task is to assign each frame with a one-hot label 𝐘 ∈ ℝ T × 3 \mathbf{Y}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times 3} , treating segmentation as a per-frame classification problem.

*Feature Representation.* To capture signing dynamics, previous work relied on HaMeR-extracted MANO hand parameters [ [55](#bib.bib55) , [64](#bib.bib64) ] and 3D pose skeletons [ [34](#bib.bib34) ] , achieving state-of-the-art segmentation [ [49](#bib.bib49) ] but at high computational costs impractical for large-scale multilingual sign datasets. We instead adopt WiLoR [ [60](#bib.bib60) ] , a MANO regression model that recently attained superior performance, while featuring a 45× faster and 32× more compact hand detector. In addition, we also introduce our segmentation model as a unimodal approach to reduce complexity and feature extraction time. This design choice was to specifically improve model efficiency, but interestingly yields no drop in performance.

*Architecture Design.* Our FAST ( F ast And A ccurate S ign segmen T ation) framework, is a transformer-based per-frame classification model ( [Fig. ˜ 2](#S3.F2) a) designed to capture hand shape transitions vital to segmentation. Specifically, given a video sequence 𝐗 = { 𝐱 1 , 𝐱 2 , ... , 𝐱 T } \bm{\mathbf{X}}=\{\mathbf{x}\_{1},\mathbf{x}\_{2},\dots,\mathbf{x}\_{T}\} we first extract per-frame MANO parameters for the left and right hands, 𝐇 L , 𝐇 R ∈ ℝ T × ( J × R ) ) \mathbf{H}\_{L},\mathbf{H}\_{R}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times(J\times R))} , where J J is the number of joints and R R the rotation dimensions. Here, we adopt 6D rotations, as it has demonstrated better suitability for deep learning [ [98](#bib.bib98) ] . To capture the independent semantic contributions of each hand, FAST encodes left and right MANO features separately via parallel streams before fusing them to predict frame-wise BIO labels. We train the network using cross-entropy (frame-level) and CTC (sign-level) alongside temporal augmentations to yield a final segmentation model that is highly scalable, accurate, and computationally efficient. Further implementation details are provided in the supplementary material.

*Keyframe Selection Policy.* Our selection policy aims to extract a sparse and semantically rich subset of frames that distills the core motion and content of each sign. Leveraging the frame-wise predictions y t ∈ ℝ 3 y\_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{3} from FAST, we ground our sampling in linguistic structure by explicitly isolating the exact temporal boundaries of each gesture: the onset ( y t = 2 y\_{t}=2 ) and the offset (defined as y t = 1 y\_{t}=1 given y t + 1 = 0 y\_{t+1}=0 ). To capture the core content of the gesture, we further incorporate the segment midpoint. This resulting policy of onset → \rightarrow mid → \rightarrow offset ( [Fig. ˜ 2](#S3.F2) b) outperforms both random and heuristically augmented baselines ( [Sec. ˜ 4.3](#S4.SS3) ), and also provides the optimal sparsity-performance trade-off (Supplementary).

### 3.2 Sparse Keyframe-conditioned Training via Flow Matching

Unlike prior SLP training approaches that condition sign generation on spoken text [ [3](#bib.bib3) ] , tokenized motion sequences [ [99](#bib.bib99) ] , or textual motion descriptions [ [5](#bib.bib5) ] , we adopt the conditioning signal of sparse keyframes and spoken text as it enables the model to learn the underlying articulatory patterns of human signing, while also respecting its linguistic reference (Fig. [3](#S3.F3) ). Formally, given a frame control signal 𝐂 ∈ ℝ T × D \mathbf{C}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times D} and text condition 𝒯 \mathcal{T} , the objective is to synthesize a signing sequence 𝐒 ∈ ℝ T × D \mathbf{S}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times D} . The control signal 𝐂 \mathbf{C} comprises of only k k keyframes ( k ≪ T k\ll T ), with the remaining ( T − k ) (T-k) frames corrupted with Gaussian noise 𝒩  ( 𝟎 , 𝐈 ) \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}) .

To represent signing sequence 𝐒 ∈ ℝ T × D \mathbf{S}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times D} , we regress SMPL-X upper-body parameters 𝐁 ∈ ℝ T × ( 11 × R ) \mathbf{B}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times(11\times R)} using NLF [ [66](#bib.bib66) ] , and MANO hand pose parameters 𝐇 L , 𝐇 R ∈ ℝ T × ( 15 × R ) \mathbf{H}\_{L},\mathbf{H}\_{R}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times(15\times R)} using WiLoR [ [60](#bib.bib60) ] .

We follow [

[99](#bib.bib99) ] by modeling the signing avatar as 11 11 upper-body joints and 15 15 joints per hand, but adopt 6D rotation parameterization [ [98](#bib.bib98) ] , resulting in a total pose dimensionality of D = 246 D=246 .

Consistent with prior work, we also maintain 𝐁 \mathbf{B} and ( 𝐇 L , 𝐇 R ) (\mathbf{H}\_{L},\mathbf{H}\_{R}) as separate channels to preserve their distinct motion dynamics. Keyframes are then obtained using the FAST model and selection policy (Sec.

[3.1](#S3.SS1) ), resulting in a binary mask of ℳ = { m 1 , m 2 , ... , m T } \mathcal{M}=\{m\_{1},m\_{2},\dots,m\_{T}\} , where m t = 1 m\_{t}=1 denotes a valid keyframe.

To enable flexible inference with or without guidance inputs 𝐂 t \mathbf{C}\_{t} and 𝒛 𝒯 {\bm{z}}\_{\mathcal{T}} , we adopt classifier-free guidance (CFG) [ [32](#bib.bib32) ] ; where during training, guidance inputs are randomly dropped with probability ρ = 0.1 \rho=0.1 , and replaced by a null token ∅ \varnothing , teaching the model to handle both keyframe conditioned and unconditioned scenarios. At inference, the guidance's effect can then be modulated by interpolating the two predicted vector fields via 𝒗 t ( γ ) = 𝒗 t uncond + γ  ( 𝒗 t cond − 𝒗 t uncond ) {\bm{v}}\_{t}^{(\gamma)}={\bm{v}}\_{t}^{\text{uncond}}+\gamma\,({\bm{v}}\_{t}^{\text{cond}}-{\bm{v}}\_{t}^{\text{uncond}}) , where 𝒗 t cond = f θ  ( t , 𝐂 t , 𝒛 𝒯 ) {\bm{v}}\_{t}^{\text{cond}}=f\_{\theta}(t,\mathbf{C}\_{t},{\bm{z}}\_{\mathcal{T}}) , 𝒗 t uncond = f θ  ( t , ∅ , ∅ ) {\bm{v}}\_{t}^{\text{uncond}}=f\_{\theta}(t,\varnothing,\varnothing) , and γ ≥ 0 \gamma\geq 0 controls the guidance scale. This formulation grants explicit control over the model's guidance adherence, and also directly enables end-to-end T2P translation at inference.

*Reconstruction Regularization.* While the CFM objective encourages the model to capture motion via vector fields, we observed that explicitly guiding the prediction towards the target pose improves convergence and sampling fidelity. To this end, we introduce a *flow-based reconstruction loss* , which forces the vector field 𝒗 t = f θ  ( t , 𝐂 t , 𝒛 𝒯 ) {\bm{v}}\_{t}=f\_{\theta}(t,\mathbf{C}\_{t},{\bm{z}}\_{\mathcal{T}}) to recover the original pose 𝒙 1 {\bm{x}}\_{1} from the noised control signal 𝐂 t \mathbf{C}\_{t} . Specifically, we estimate the original pose via a single-step Euler integration along the vector field from the current timestep t t towards t = 1 t=1 :

|    | 𝒙  1  est  =  𝒙  t  +  (  1  −  t  )  𝒗  t  ,  t  ∈  [  0  ,  1  ]  .  {\bm{x}}\_{1}^{\mathrm{est}}={\bm{x}}\_{t}+(1-t)\,{\bm{v}}\_{t},\quad t\in[0,1].   |    | (4)   |
|----|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----|-------|

We then supervise the network using mean-squared error to penalize significant deviations from the ground-truth poses:

|    | ℒ  recon  =  𝔼  𝒙  1  ,  𝒙  t  ,  t  ‖  𝒙  1  est  −  𝒙  1  ‖  2  .  \mathcal{L}\_{\text{recon}}=\mathbb{E}\_{{\bm{x}}\_{1},{\bm{x}}\_{t},t}\big\&#124;{\bm{x}}\_{1}^{\text{est}}-{\bm{x}}\_{1}\big\&#124;^{2}.   |    | (5)   |
|----|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----|-------|

Crucially, this reconstruction loss optimizes the vector field for *one-step sampling* , enabling the extreme inference efficiency of SignSparK. To enforce temporal coherence and smooth dynamics, we further introduce a *velocity-matching loss* that aligns the predicted inter-frame displacements with the ground truth:

|    | ℒ  vel  =  𝔼  𝒙  1  ,  𝒙  t  ,  t  ‖  (  𝒙  1  ,  f  +  1  est  −  𝒙  1  ,  f  est  )  −  (  𝒙  1  ,  f  +  1  −  𝒙  1  ,  f  )  ‖  2  ,  \mathcal{L}\_{\text{vel}}=\mathbb{E}\_{{\bm{x}}\_{1},{\bm{x}}\_{t},t}\big\&#124;({\bm{x}}\_{1,f+1}^{\text{est}}-{\bm{x}}\_{1,f}^{\text{est}})-({\bm{x}}\_{1,f+1}-{\bm{x}}\_{1,f})\big\&#124;^{2},   |    | (6)   |
|----|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----|-------|

with f f indexing the consecutive frames in the motion sequence. The final objective is then the combination between the CFM and reconstruction losses:

|    | ℒ  SignSparK  =  λ  CFM  ℒ  CFM  +  λ  recon  ℒ  recon  +  λ  vel  ℒ  vel  ,  \mathcal{L}\_{\text{SignSparK}}=\lambda\_{\text{CFM}}\mathcal{L}\_{\text{CFM}}+\lambda\_{\text{recon}}\mathcal{L}\_{\text{recon}}+\lambda\_{\text{vel}}\mathcal{L}\_{\text{vel}},   |    | (7)   |
|----|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----|-------|

where λ recon \lambda\_{\text{recon}} and λ vel \lambda\_{\text{vel}} weight the auxiliary terms. By explicitly reconstructing the target pose at each timestep via one-step integrations, SignSparK consequently learns more faithful and fluid signing motion, improving generation quality ( [Sec. ˜ 4.3](#S4.SS3) ) while requiring far fewer sampling steps.

## 4 Experiments

In this section, we evaluate both of our main contributions: the sign language segmentor FAST and the SLP framework SignSparK . We first outline the datasets and evaluation metrics used, followed by state-of-the-art comparisons, ablations, and qualitative results. As human evaluation remains the gold standard for assessing sign correctness, we also include a comprehensive user study.

*Datasets.* For sign segmentation, we follow standard practice [ [52](#bib.bib52) , [51](#bib.bib51) , [49](#bib.bib49) ] and adopt MeineDGS [ [42](#bib.bib42) ] as both our training and evaluation benchmark. This corpus offers precise frame-level boundary annotations for continuous signing, making it the only resource suitable for this task. Nonetheless, we show that FAST generalizes well even to unseen signers and datasets in the supplementary.

For SLP, we construct a multilingual training corpus following SOKE [ [99](#bib.bib99) ] by merging Phoenix14T [ [10](#bib.bib10) ] , CSLDaily [ [97](#bib.bib97) ] , and How2Sign [ [21](#bib.bib21) ] . However, we further extend their setup by adding British Sign Language data from the large-scale BOBSL corpus [ [1](#bib.bib1) ] , enhancing linguistic diversity and coverage. To prevent data dominance, only 10% of BOBSL is used for training. For downstream evaluations, SignSparK is rigorously benchmarked across multiple SLP regimes. To ensure fair comparison with prior work, we align our test sets accordingly: (i) Sign Stitching (Gloss-to-Pose, G2P) is evaluated on Phoenix14T, MeineDGS, and BSLCorpus [ [18](#bib.bib18) ] following [ [84](#bib.bib84) ] , while (ii) the Text-to-Pose (T2P) and Keyframe-to-Pose (KF2P) tasks are assessed on Phoenix14T, CSLDaily, and How2Sign, following [ [99](#bib.bib99) ] . Additional dataset statistics are provided in the supplementary.

*Evaluation Metrics.* To evaluate segmentation performance, we follow the metrics adopted in prior work [ [52](#bib.bib52) , [51](#bib.bib51) , [49](#bib.bib49) ] . Specifically, we report frame-level F1 score (F1) for BIO label accuracy, Intersection over Union (IoU) to measure temporal overlap between predicted and ground-truth segments, and the Segment Ratio (SR) to quantify over- or under-segmentation based on segment counts.

Meanwhile, to evaluate performance across SLP regimes, we employ task-specific protocols. For Sign Stitching, we follow [ [84](#bib.bib84) ] , utilizing Dynamic Time Warping on joint positions (DTW-JPE) to evaluate motion coherence and a Back-Translation (B-T) model to measure semantic intelligibility. For the T2P and KF2P tasks, we adopt the protocol of SOKE [ [99](#bib.bib99) ] . Here, motion and spatial fidelity are measured via DTW errors on both Procrustes-aligned (DTW-PA-JPE) and unaligned (DTW-JPE) joint positions. For KF2P, we also report B-T BLEU-4 scores [ [53](#bib.bib53) ] using the SL-Transformer model [ [11](#bib.bib11) ] , consistent with most prior approaches [ [68](#bib.bib68) , [33](#bib.bib33) , [84](#bib.bib84) , [70](#bib.bib70) , [71](#bib.bib71) ] . However, since BLEU scores vary heavily based on B-T configurations, they are not safely comparable across independent studies. We therefore additionally report *relative* BLEU-4 scores, computed as the percentage drop from the ground-truth, for a more interpretable comparison. To ensure fair comparisons, we will include our B-T model in the code release.
